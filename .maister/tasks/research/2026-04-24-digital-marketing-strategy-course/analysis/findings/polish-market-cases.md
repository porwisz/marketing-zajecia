### CASE STUDY 1: Allegro — "Czego szukasz w Święta?" / "English for Beginners"

#### Company Background

Allegro is Poland's dominant e-commerce marketplace, founded in 1999 and headquartered in Poznań. As of 2024 it holds approximately 45-50% of Poland's total e-commerce market share, with 15.2 million active buyers in Poland and Gross Merchandise Value of 15.3 billion EUR in 2024 (10% growth YoY). Over 70% of Poles consider Allegro synonymous with online retail. The platform is 99% third-party marketplace business — a Polish answer to Amazon.

Sources: [Allegro statistics — Statista](https://www.statista.com/topics/11893/allegro/), [Allegro CEE Expansion overview](https://www.marketmaze.me/p/allegro-cee-growth-marketplace-ai-2025), [E-commerce w Polsce 2024 — Gemius/PBI/IAB Polska via iment.pl](https://iment.pl/e-commerce-w-polsce-2024-kluczowe-dane-i-wnioski-z-raportu-gemius-pbi-oraz-iab-polska/)

#### Strategic Challenge / Opportunity

As an e-commerce platform, Allegro competes primarily on price and selection — a low-differentiation position. The challenge: how to build genuine brand love, not just transaction volumes, in a market where Amazon and international players were entering. Allegro needed to shift from "cheapest" to "meaningful" — to own an emotional territory that a pure price-comparison platform cannot occupy.

#### What They Did Digitally

Allegro partnered with Warsaw-based creative agency **Bardzo** to launch the campaign series "Czego szukasz w Święta?" ("What are you looking for this Christmas?"), starting in 2016. The approach:

- **Emotional brand storytelling**: Each year's 3-minute online film tells a human story that barely features the product. The 2016 film "English for Beginners" depicts an elderly Polish grandfather secretly learning English to surprise his grandchild in the UK. Allegro appears only as the tool that enables this — he buys language books on Allegro.
- **YouTube-first distribution**: The films were released on YouTube and distributed organically via social sharing — no paid TV campaign was the primary driver.
- **Serialised brand narrative**: The "Czego szukasz?" series continued annually (2017, 2018, 2019...), building a recognisable brand signature. A 2019 continuation brought the grandfather back.
- **Earned media amplification**: Rather than spending on reach, Allegro relied on press coverage and social shares to distribute internationally.
- **Platform as silent hero**: The creative philosophy — never show the product as the star, show the human need it enables — became the brand's distinctive positioning.

#### Results with Specific Metrics

- **"English for Beginners" (2016)**: Over 6 million YouTube views within the first week of release; over 11 million views within two weeks ([CNBC coverage, December 2016](https://www.cnbc.com/2016/12/12/polish-online-retailers-christmas-tearjerker-ad-gets-11-million-views.html)); total views reached approximately 12.77 million on the primary upload.
- **International comparison**: The ad was on track to challenge John Lewis's Christmas ad "Buster the Boxer" which had 22.5 million views — a remarkable result for a Polish-language retailer.
- **International press coverage**: Covered by CNBC, Refinery29, Bored Panda, The New East Digital Archive, and dozens of international media outlets. The film was shared globally without subtitles — demonstrating the power of visual storytelling.
- **Awards**: The series won a Silver at the 30th Epica Awards (international advertising competition) for an earlier spot in the series ("Gitara"), and the campaign has been consistently recognised in Polish marketing industry awards.
- **Brand awareness**: Allegro holds 88% brand awareness among Polish online shoppers in 2024 (+2 pp vs 2023), the highest of any e-commerce platform in Poland.
- **Advertising revenue**: Allegro's advertising revenue grew by 32% in 2024 ([AIM Group, 2025](https://aimgroup.com/2025/04/29/allegros-advertising-revenue-surges-by-32-in-2024/)), partly reflecting the strength of the brand equity built through content like this.

Sources: [CNBC — 11 million views](https://www.cnbc.com/2016/12/12/polish-online-retailers-christmas-tearjerker-ad-gets-11-million-views.html), [Refinery29 — campaign overview](https://www.refinery29.com/en-us/2016/12/132181/allegro-holiday-commercial-english-for-beginners), [socialpress.pl — Polish analysis](https://socialpress.pl/en/2016/11/czego-szukasz-w-swieta-wzruszajaca-kampania-od-allegro/), [ecommercowy.pl — ecommerce context](https://ecommercowy.pl/spot-allegro-czego-szukasz-w-swieta-czyli-e-commerce-w-boze-narodzenie/)

#### Key Lesson for Students

**Brand equity is built through values, not features.** Allegro does not advertise lower prices in these films. It advertises the human motivations behind shopping: love, belonging, connection. This is "Jobs to Be Done" thinking applied to brand communication — Allegro understands customers don't want to "buy language books," they want to connect with their grandchild. The digital distribution (YouTube-first, earned media dependent) was not a budget constraint but a strategic choice: emotional content spreads when it is genuinely moving, not when it is paid for.

The series also illustrates that a content strategy can build a consistent brand platform across years — each annual film reinforces the same emotional territory even with a different story.

#### Discussion Questions

1. Allegro's campaign barely shows the product and never mentions price. How does this create more durable brand differentiation than a "lowest price" message? What risks does this approach carry?

2. The film went viral internationally despite being in Polish with no subtitles. What does this tell us about the hierarchy of emotional vs. rational communication in digital content?

### CASE STUDY 2: Żabka — Żappka App & Digital-Physical Integration

#### Company Background

Żabka is Poland's largest convenience store chain with over 10,000 locations across Poland (density: more than 700 million customer visits per year). Founded in 1998 and majority owned by CVC Capital Partners. The brand operates on a franchise model. Historically it was a purely physical retailer with no digital presence — the challenge of digitalisation was not creating a new business but transforming the experience of a physical one.

Sources: [Żabka Group — Żappka page](https://zabkagroup.com/convenience/zappka/), [FutureMind Żappka case study](https://www.futuremind.com/case-studies/zabka-zappka-superapp/)

#### Strategic Challenge / Opportunity

Convenience retail is commoditised — every Żabka sells the same products at roughly the same price. The opportunity was to use data and mobile technology to: (a) personalise promotions to individual customers, (b) increase visit frequency, (c) build loyalty in a sector where none traditionally existed, and (d) position Żabka as an innovator, not just a corner shop.

#### What They Did Digitally

**Phase 1 — Żappka loyalty app (launched ~2019):**
- Built on a custom platform developed by FutureMind, using Azure cloud, native iOS/Android, Node.js backend
- Integrated with Synerise for real-time personalisation and marketing automation
- Core mechanics: customers earn "żappsy" (loyalty points) redeemable for rewards; the app delivers personalised promotions based on individual purchase history
- App-exclusive offers unavailable in-store incentivise download and usage
- Infobip integration for SMS and push communication

**Phase 2 — Hyper-personalisation:**
- AI-powered offer engine tailors promotions to each user based on shopping behaviour — "state-of-the-art data analytics" that adapts across Żabka's entire ecosystem
- Geolocation triggers: proximity-based notifications when a customer is near a store
- Shopping challenges gamify purchasing behaviour (buy X to earn bonus żappsy)

**Phase 3 — Żappka Pay and Żabka Nano:**
- Żappka Pay: mobile payment function that simultaneously applies loyalty points and personalised discounts, making the checkout seamless and the data loop complete
- **Żabka Nano**: fully autonomous stores (no staff, no checkout) where customers open the door with the Żappka app or a payment card, take products, and are charged automatically via computer vision (AiFi AI) + Microsoft Cloud for Retail
- Nano stores operate 24/7 and have been deployed in gyms, universities, transit stations, and factories (e.g. Michelin)

Sources: [FutureMind — Żappka superapp case study](https://www.futuremind.com/case-studies/zabka-zappka-superapp/), [Microsoft Customer Story — Żabka Nano](https://www.microsoft.com/en/customers/story/1589693036635058141-zabka-retail-microsoft-cloud-for-retail), [Adyen — Żabka autonomous stores](https://www.adyen.com/knowledge-hub/how-zabka-is-redefining-convenience-with-autonomous-stores)

#### Results with Specific Metrics

- **Żappka app downloads**: 10+ million total downloads
- **Active users**: 4 million regular active users
- **National reach**: App used by nearly one-third of all people in Poland
- **Nano stores NPS**: Net Promoter Score of 80+ (vs. industry average of ~50 for regular retail)
- **Products sold via Nano technology**: Close to 2 million products sold through autonomous technology in first year of operation
- **Nano store network**: Became the largest autonomous store chain in Europe (50+ locations), with 1,000+ planned in next 12-24 months
- **Awards**: Mobile Trends Awards 2019 — winner in Commerce/M-commerce category

Sources: [FutureMind case study](https://www.futuremind.com/case-studies/zabka-zappka-superapp/), [Microsoft Customer Story](https://www.microsoft.com/en/customers/story/1589693036635058141-zabka-retail-microsoft-cloud-for-retail), [Whitelabel Loyalty — Top loyalty programs Poland 2025](https://whitelabel-loyalty.com/blog/loyalty/top-loyalty-programs-in-poland-2025/), [PR Newswire — 25 Nano stores milestone](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/abka-nano-autonomous-stores-powered-by-aifi-outpace-industry-with-25-live-locations-301462392.html)

#### Key Lesson for Students

**Digital transformation in physical retail is about turning the shop visit into a data event.** Every Żappka scan creates a data point that feeds personalisation, which increases the value of the next promotion, which increases the likelihood of the next visit. This is a **loyalty flywheel**: data → personalisation → relevance → frequency → more data.

The Żabka Nano case further illustrates how digital CVP (customer value proposition) can eliminate friction entirely, converting a transaction that used to require a human cashier into a fully automated experience. The integration of the Żappka app into the Nano door-unlock mechanism means loyalty and convenience are inseparable — a textbook example of "digital as product."

#### Discussion Questions

1. Żabka's Żappka app offers personalised discounts in exchange for behavioural data. What is the ethical boundary between personalisation and surveillance in retail loyalty programmes?

2. The Nano store concept requires customers to trust that they will be charged correctly by an AI system. How does Żabka build this trust, and what role does brand reputation play in enabling new technology adoption?

### CASE STUDY 3: InPost — Paczkomat as Digital-Physical CVP

#### Company Background

InPost is a Polish logistics company founded by Rafał Brzoska in 1999 in Kraków. The company invented the Paczkomat (automated parcel locker) concept and introduced it in Poland in 2009. InPost is now listed on Euronext Amsterdam (IPO January 2021, raising over €2.8 billion — one of the largest European tech IPOs of that year). In 2024, InPost handled more than 1 billion shipments across 9 countries.

Sources: [InPost — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/InPost), [Poland Insight — 1 billion parcels](https://polandinsight.com/inpost-group-delivers-over-one-billion-parcels-in-2024-for-the-first-time-92793/)

#### Strategic Challenge / Opportunity

In the early 2010s, Polish e-commerce was growing rapidly but was constrained by the frustrations of home delivery: missed deliveries, re-delivery fees, inflexible time windows. InPost identified this as a structural CVP problem — not a logistics problem but a customer experience problem. The insight: customers don't want "delivery to the door," they want "their package, on their terms." Parcel lockers — open 24/7, near their home, no waiting — solve this better than any courier.

The second challenge was brand building: InPost needed to make its network a trusted, emotionally positive brand, not just infrastructure.

#### What They Did Digitally

**CVP differentiation through the network itself:**
- Invested aggressively in network density: today over 25,000 Paczkomat machines in Poland, with approximately 90% of urban residents within a 7-minute walk
- The accessibility of the network became a marketing asset — "7 minut od domu" (7 minutes from home) became a brand message

**Digital brand communication:**
- Human-to-Human (H2H) brand voice: InPost communicates in a simple, direct, humorous style that treats customers as partners — rare for a logistics company
- Brand mascot "Mat" (formerly "Paczucha") gives the brand personality
- Influencer partnerships and social contests (e.g. inviting customers to design graphics for Paczkomat machines)
- Content marketing: 334 published articles covering logistics, e-commerce, and business topics
- Real-time marketing: InPost has been cited in academic research as one of Poland's most effective practitioners of real-time social media marketing

**InPost mobile app and digital services:**
- 13.7 million app users as of 2024
- InPost Pay: digital payment and loyalty programme integrating the shipping experience with e-commerce
- Push notifications replacing physical delivery attempts — customers are notified the moment a parcel arrives

**Digital marketing technology:**
- Retargeting and performance advertising to reach e-commerce shoppers at the moment of purchase decision
- InPost's article on "digital marketing and new advertising technology" describes their use of programmatic and data-driven targeting

Sources: [InPost — digital marketing article](https://inpost.pl/en/news-digital-marketing-few-words-about-new-advertising-technology), [Alsendo — parcel locker revolution](https://alsendo.com/alsendo-insights-polands-parcel-locker-revolution/), [We Did It In Poland — Paczkomat innovation](https://wediditinpoland.eu/en/innowacja/paczkomat-inpost/), [InPost — 1000ideas marketing portfolio](https://1000i.pl/en/portfolio/inpost/)

#### Results with Specific Metrics

- **Market share**: 88% of parcel locker users in Poland choose InPost (-5 pp from dominant 93% in prior years, as competition enters)
- **Consumer preference**: 94% of Polish online shoppers who use lockers choose InPost Paczkomat
- **Network size**: 47,000 Paczkomat devices across 9 countries; over 25,000 in Poland alone
- **Volume growth 2024**: Parcel volumes to Paczkomat machines grew 103% YoY in 2024
- **Total parcels handled**: 1 billion+ parcels in 2024 (first time in company history)
- **App users**: 13.7 million
- **International expansion**: More Paczkomat machines outside Poland than inside (as of 2024)
- **Consumer adoption driver**: 84% of Polish consumers say they are more likely to shop online because of convenient parcel locker access

Sources: [Alsendo — parcel locker data](https://alsendo.com/alsendo-insights-polands-parcel-locker-revolution/), [We Did It In Poland](https://wediditinpoland.eu/en/innowacja/paczkomat-inpost/), [Poland Insight — 1 billion parcels](https://polandinsight.com/inpost-group-delivers-over-one-billion-parcels-in-2024-for-the-first-time-92793/), [trans.info — 2024 records](https://trans.info/en/inpost-2024-records-407912), [E-commerce w Polsce 2024](https://iment.pl/e-commerce-w-polsce-2024-kluczowe-dane-i-wnioski-z-raportu-gemius-pbi-oraz-iab-polska/)

#### Key Lesson for Students

**The CVP IS the marketing.** InPost barely needed a traditional advertising campaign in its growth phase — the product itself, when experienced, converted customers into advocates. A Paczkomat that works reliably at 11pm on Sunday is a better advertisement than any TV spot.

This case illustrates the concept of **product-led growth**: when the product delivers a genuinely superior experience, adoption becomes self-reinforcing. Every online retailer that offered InPost as a delivery option was effectively endorsing and promoting InPost. InPost built a distribution network where distribution partners (online shops) did the customer acquisition.

The brand communication layer (mascot, H2H voice, social media) then reinforced this functional advantage with emotional warmth — making InPost feel like a friend who holds your parcels, not a corporation.

#### Discussion Questions

1. InPost's core competitive advantage is physical infrastructure (parcel lockers), not digital technology. Yet it is described as a digital brand. Where exactly is the "digital" in InPost's CVP?

2. InPost's market share has begun to slip as competitors (ORLEN Paczka, Allegro One Box) enter the parcel locker market. What digital marketing strategies would you recommend for InPost to defend its dominant position?

### CASE STUDY 4: CD Projekt Red — Community-Driven Global Brand

#### Company Background

CD Projekt Red (CDPR) is a Polish video game developer founded in Warsaw in 1994 by Marcin Iwiński and Michał Kiciński, originally as a games distributor. The company created the Wiedźmin (Witcher) game series based on Andrzej Sapkowski's Polish fantasy novels, starting in 2007. CDPR also founded GOG.com (Good Old Games), a DRM-free digital game distribution platform. The company is listed on the Warsaw Stock Exchange.

CDPR represents one of the most successful Polish global brands of the 21st century — built primarily through digital channels, community engagement, and cultural content, not mass advertising.

Sources: [CD Projekt — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CD_Projekt), [CD Projekt annual report 2023](https://www.cdprojekt.com/en/wp-content/uploads-en/2024/03/management-board-report-on-cd-projekt-group-activities-in-2023.pdf)

#### Strategic Challenge / Opportunity

When CDPR made The Witcher (2007), it was an unknown Polish studio adapting a beloved Polish literary franchise for global audiences. The challenge: how does a small studio with limited marketing budget compete with EA, Ubisoft, and Activision for global attention? The answer was to build a community that would do much of the marketing for them — and to give that community genuine reasons to trust and advocate for the brand.

#### What They Did Digitally

**Values-based brand positioning:**
- **DRM-free philosophy**: CDPR released its games without digital rights management — a radical, customer-friendly position when competitors used increasingly aggressive copy-protection. GOG.com was founded explicitly on this principle. This generated enormous goodwill and free press coverage.
- **Free DLC model**: CDPR released 16 pieces of free downloadable content for The Witcher 3 — again, against industry norms. This was a deliberate marketing strategy, generating press coverage and reinforcing the brand's "player-first" positioning.
- **Direct community relations**: CDPR maintained "direct relations with the player base via electronic and social media channels," including responding to fans, sharing development behind-the-scenes, and building anticipation through organic community activity.

**Long-form pre-launch campaign for The Witcher 3:**
- Extended multi-year marketing campaign running from announcement to launch in 2015
- Multiple deep-dive trailers, gameplay demonstrations, developer interviews, and press previews
- Strategy: "The company's preference leans toward long-term marketing campaigns... maximise reach, generate pre-sales and create deep engagement with the fan base before the official launch."
- Coverage across gaming YouTube channels, Twitch streamers, Reddit communities, and specialist press

**Social media and multi-platform presence:**
- TikTok: 270,000 followers, with #cyberpunk2077 generating 3.6 billion global views
- Strong presence across Twitter/X, Instagram, YouTube, Reddit (/r/witcher, /r/cyberpunkgame)
- CDPR officially describes TikTok as "one of their key biddable media platforms"

**Cyberpunk 2077 — crisis marketing and recovery:**
- Initial launch (December 2020) was marred by performance issues on PS4/Xbox One
- CDPR's recovery strategy: transparent public apologies, free patches, refund offers — maintaining the "honest developer" brand even in failure
- Partnership with anime studio Trigger to produce "Cyberpunk: Edgerunners" (Netflix, 2022) — critical hit that revitalised interest in the game without a paid advertising campaign
- The Phantom Liberty expansion (2023): 10 million copies sold; game won "Best Ongoing Game" (2023)

**Netflix effect — cross-media amplification:**
- The Witcher TV series (Netflix, launched December 2019) caused a **554% increase in The Witcher 3 game sales** in December 2019 vs December 2018 — demonstrating the power of content IP across media.

Sources: [Cyberpunk 2077 digital marketing analysis — Alcel Labs](https://alcelabs.com/video-games/cyberpunk-digital-marketing-strategy/), [TikTok for Business — CD Projekt Red case study](https://ads.tiktok.com/business/en-US/inspiration/cd-projekt-red-cyberpunk-2077-phantom-liberty), [ICS Digital — Cyberpunk marketing analysis](https://www.ics-digital.com/blog/cyberpunk-2077), [The Witcher 3 Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Witcher_3:_Wild_Hunt)

#### Results with Specific Metrics

- **The Witcher 3 total sales**: 60 million copies (as of 2024/2025), across a decade since 2015 launch — [Shacknews](https://www.shacknews.com/article/144443/witcher-3-60-million-copies-sold)
- **Revenue**: Approximately 2.4 billion PLN (~$688 million) in lifetime revenue from The Witcher 3 alone
- **Pre-launch cost recovery**: Development costs were fully recouped from pre-order sales alone
- **Netflix synergy**: 554% increase in Witcher 3 game sales following Netflix series launch
- **Cyberpunk 2077**: 20 million units sold in 2022; Phantom Liberty expansion exceeded 10 million sales
- **#cyberpunk2077 TikTok**: 3.6 billion global views
- **GOG.com**: Built a platform with tens of millions of users based on values-based marketing (DRM-free, player-friendly)
- **Brand recognition**: Polish studio recognised globally as a AAA creative force — a brand that competes on storytelling and trust, not marketing spend

Sources: [Witcher 3 — 60 million copies — Shacknews](https://www.shacknews.com/article/144443/witcher-3-60-million-copies-sold), [Tweaktown — sales and revenue](https://www.tweaktown.com/news/105504/witcher-3-reaches-60-million-game-sales-but-may-not-have-broken-1-billion-revenue/index.html), [VGChartz — milestones](https://www.vgchartz.com/article/464798/the-witcher-3-sales-top-60-million-units-cyberpunk-2077-phantom-liberty-sales-top-10-million-units/)

#### Key Lesson for Students

**Brand values expressed through product decisions are the most credible marketing.** CDPR did not just say "we respect gamers" — it proved it by releasing free DLC, going DRM-free, and offering refunds after a flawed launch. Each of these decisions generated more authentic brand advocacy than any advertising campaign could.

This case also demonstrates **community as marketing infrastructure**: when a brand has a devoted community of millions of fans on Reddit, YouTube, TikTok, and Discord, new product launches have a built-in distribution network that requires no paid media. The challenge — as Cyberpunk 2077's troubled launch showed — is that community trust, once damaged, requires genuine product and communications effort to rebuild, not just PR.

The Netflix effect illustrates **IP amplification**: a strong narrative world, when extended into other media, can drive sales of existing products. For marketers, this raises the concept of content ecosystems — brands as publishers across multiple platforms.

#### Discussion Questions

1. CDPR's "no DRM / free DLC" philosophy cost money in the short term. How would you make the business case to a CFO that customer-friendly policies are a form of marketing investment with measurable ROI?

2. The Cyberpunk 2077 launch showed that community trust, built over years, can be severely damaged in days. What crisis communication lessons can be drawn from CDPR's response, and what worked in the recovery?

### CASE STUDY 5: mBank — Pioneer of Digital-First Banking in Poland

#### Company Background

mBank was launched on the night of 25-26 November 2000 by BRE Bank as Poland's first fully internet-based bank — no physical branches, all transactions online or by phone. This was a radical innovation in Polish banking at the time, two years before most Poles had broadband internet at home. mBank is now the fourth-largest universal banking group in Poland by total assets (~246 billion PLN in 2024), serving 5.7 million retail clients and 36,000 corporate clients.

Sources: [mBank Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MBank), [mBank newsroom — icon of mobility](https://en.media.mbank.pl/36500-mbank-from-poland-the-icon-of-mobility), [International Banker — mBank digital leadership](https://internationalbanker.com/banking/mbank-leading-the-new-wave-of-innovation-digitalisation-and-competitiveness-in-polish-banking/)

#### Strategic Challenge / Opportunity

In 2013, mBank undertook a complete digital reinvention of its customer platform — launching a new interface and brand positioning to remain competitive as mobile banking emerged. The challenge was not just technical but marketing: how to rebuild a brand's digital identity and acquire new customers through digital channels alone, with no branch network for physical marketing.

#### What They Did Digitally

**2000-2013 — Pioneer phase:**
- Operated entirely without branches from founding; all acquisition was digital (web, email, online advertising)
- Achieved 18,000 customers in initial months purely through digital channels — unprecedented in Polish banking
- Built brand equity as the "young person's bank" — modern, cheap, no queues

**2013 — Full platform reinvention:**
- New mBank launched with a completely redesigned digital platform and brand identity
- Marketing result: 45% of new account openings in the post-launch period were directly linked to interactions with marketing content for the new mBank (source: MIT CISR case study on mBank)
- Main website mBank.pl generated 60%+ of traffic via direct visitors — evidence of strong brand awareness and organic pull

**Mobile-first leadership:**
- July 2017: Mobile logins exceeded computer logins for the first time — a milestone celebrated publicly as part of brand narrative
- September 2019: Mobile accounted for 68.6% of all account logins
- As of 2024: 3.7 million active mobile banking users

**Digital marketing and gamification:**
- "Mgra" — Poland's first bank gamification solution, integrated into online banking
- Facebook-integrated money transfers (sending money via social network)
- Social media: mBank generates significantly more website traffic from social media than its European banking peers (Forrester/MIT CISR comparison)
- Content marketing: financial education content, targeting young digital-native customers

**Digital brand metrics:**
- Won 12+ global innovation awards since February 2013, including Finovate "Best of Show" awards in Europe and the US
- Ranked in top 20 EMEA banks in Deloitte's 2018 Digital Banking Maturity study
- Euromoney Best Digital Bank for SMEs in Poland (2025)
- Poland itself ranked by Deloitte (2018) as a "digital leader" market — ahead of France and Norway — with mBank as a key contributor

Sources: [International Banker — mBank](https://internationalbanker.com/banking/mbank-leading-the-new-wave-of-innovation-digitalisation-and-competitiveness-in-polish-banking/), [MIT CISR — mBank Creating the Digital Bank](https://cisr.mit.edu/publication/2015_1001_mBank_FonstadWoernerWeill), [Forrester case study (abstract)](https://www.forrester.com/report/Case+Study+mBank+Builds+A+NextGeneration+Online+Banking+Experience/-/E-RES106321), [mBank — About us](https://www.mbank.pl/en/about-us/about-mbank/)

#### Results with Specific Metrics

- **Customer growth**: From 0 to 18,000 customers in first months (2000); to 4.9 million retail customers by 2015; to 5.7 million in 2024
- **New customer acquisition via digital content (2013 relaunch)**: 45% of new account openings linked to marketing content interactions
- **Direct web traffic**: 60%+ of mBank.pl traffic via direct visitors (high brand awareness indicator)
- **Mobile milestone**: Mobile logins overtook desktop in July 2017 — four months before this occurred at most major European banks
- **Active mobile users**: 3.7 million as of 2024
- **Awards**: 12+ global banking innovation awards since 2013; Euromoney Best Digital Bank SMEs Poland 2025
- **Market context**: Poland ranked as a European "digital leader" in banking by Deloitte — mBank was a primary reason

#### Key Lesson for Students

**Being first in a digital channel is a durable competitive advantage** — but only if you maintain the commitment. mBank was 13 years ahead of the Polish banking market in going digital-only. That early advantage created a brand positioning ("the smart person's bank, the bank without queues") that persists decades later.

mBank also demonstrates that **digital marketing and product innovation are inseparable in financial services**. The marketing story of mBank cannot be told without describing the product: the mobile-first banking experience, the gamification, the social media integrations. The brand and the product reinforce each other.

The 2013 reinvention offers a textbook example of **brand relaunch via digital content**: when 45% of new account openings are attributable to marketing content interactions, you have demonstrated that content marketing creates measurable business outcomes — not just brand awareness.

#### Discussion Questions

1. mBank launched with no physical branches in 2000 — a decision that was considered risky. How has the rise of mobile banking vindicated or complicated this original strategic choice?

2. mBank uses gamification (Mgra) and social media integration (Facebook transfers) in its banking product. Where is the line between "innovative user experience" and "making banking addictive"? What are the ethical implications?

## PART 2 — POLISH DIGITAL MARKET LANDSCAPE

### 2.1 Internet Usage in Poland (2024-2025)

**Population**: 38.4 million
**Internet users**: 34.5 million (89.8% penetration) — source: [DataReportal Digital 2025 Poland](https://datareportal.com/reports/digital-2025-poland)
**Social media user identities**: 29.0 million (75.6% of population); grew by 1.1 million (+3.9%) in 2024
**Still offline**: ~3.91 million people

**Mobile internet:**
- 53.7 million mobile connections (140% of population — reflecting multi-SIM usage)
- Mobile internet download speed: 69.09 Mbps median (+64% YoY — major infrastructure improvement)
- Fixed internet speed: 178.10 Mbps median
- 91% of internet users access via phone or tablet (Gemius/PBI Mediapanel, Q4 2024)
- Mobile applications account for 65% of total time spent online; desktop websites 24%; mobile browser 9%

**Time spent:**
- Average daily internet usage: 8 hours 22 minutes per person
- Average daily mobile usage: 6 hours 12 minutes
- Average daily social media: approximately 2 hours

### 2.3 Polish E-Commerce Market

**Online shoppers**: 78% of internet users regularly shop online (approximately 23.4 million people, 2024)
**Foreign shopping growth**: 35% of online shoppers buy from abroad (+5 pp vs 2023)

**Market size estimates:**
- Polish e-commerce market value reached 180 billion PLN in 2023 (+17% YoY) — [Gemius/PBI/IAB Polska E-commerce Report 2024](https://pbi.org.pl/patronaty/zakupy-zagraniczne-produkty-uzywane-i-platnosci-blikiem-raport-e-commerce-w-polsce-2024/)
- International forecasts: ~USD 26.7 billion in 2026, growing at CAGR 7.57% to USD 38.5 billion by 2031 — [Technavio](https://www.technavio.com/report/poland-e-commerce-market-analysis)

**Market leaders:**
- **Allegro**: 88% brand awareness; ~45-50% of total Polish e-commerce market share; 15.2 million active buyers; GMV 15.3 billion EUR (2024)
- **Top categories**: Fashion (74%), footwear (65%), cosmetics/beauty (62%)
- Competition: Amazon.pl, Empik, eBay, AliExpress, Zalando

**Delivery preferences (2024):**
- Parcel lockers: 81% primary preference (down 1 pp vs 2023)
- InPost: 88% of locker users select InPost
- Home delivery courier: declining preference
- 84% of consumers say convenient parcel lockers make them more likely to shop online

**Payment preferences (2024):**
- BLIK: 68% (+5 pp vs 2023; +10 pp vs 2022) — now Poland's dominant payment method
- Online bank transfer: 64% (declining)
- Payment cards: stable

**Note on BLIK**: BLIK is a Polish mobile payment standard (launched 2015 by Polish Payment Standard, a consortium of Polish banks). It is now the default payment method in Polish e-commerce and peer-to-peer payments — a uniquely Polish fintech success story.
