# Course Design & Facilitation Best Practices
## 8-Hour Hybrid Lecture-Workshop Day: Digital Marketing Strategy for Postgraduate Students

**Research date**: 2026-04-24
**Research question**: What are the best practices for designing and facilitating an 8-hour hybrid lecture-workshop day on digital marketing strategy for postgraduate students?

---

## 1. Adult Learning Principles (Andragogy)

### Knowles' Six Core Assumptions

Malcolm Knowles identified six assumptions that must drive instructional design for adult learners. Each has a direct implication for an 8-hour course day.

| Assumption | What It Means | Design Implication |
|---|---|---|
| **Need to know** | Adults need to understand WHY before they learn | Open every lecture block with a 2-minute "why this matters to you" framing |
| **Self-concept** | Adults see themselves as self-directed learners | Offer choices within exercises (pick a brand, pick a focus area) |
| **Prior experience** | Adults bring professional knowledge as a resource | Use peer-sharing rounds; ask "who has seen this?" before explaining |
| **Readiness to learn** | Adults engage when content solves a current problem | Anchor theory to real brands students work with or know |
| **Problem orientation** | Adults want learning that addresses real challenges | Case study + framework application — not theory in isolation |
| **Intrinsic motivation** | Confidence and career growth drive adults | Frame exercises as professional skill-building, not tests |

**Source**: [eLearning Industry — Adult Learning Theory: Andragogy of Malcolm Knowles](https://elearningindustry.com/the-adult-learning-theory-andragogy-of-malcolm-knowles)

### Lecture-to-Workshop Ratio

The **70:20:10 model** suggests adults learn:
- **70%** from doing (on-the-job, hands-on practice)
- **20%** from social learning (peers, discussion, feedback)
- **10%** from formal instruction (lecture, structured content)

For a workshop day, this translates to an approximate **30% lecture / 70% activity** split within formal teaching time. In practice for an 8-hour day:
- ~2–2.5 hours of direct instruction (lecture slides, concept explanation)
- ~4.5–5 hours of workshop activities, exercises, discussion, and reflection
- ~1 hour in breaks

**Key principle**: Andragogy focuses on the *process* of learning, not the product. Design for discovery and construction of understanding through problem-solving and peer collaboration.

**Source**: [Innovation Training — 70-20-10 Model for Learning and Development](https://www.innovationtraining.org/70-20-10-model-for-learning-and-development/)

### Engagement Techniques for Professional Adult Learners

- **Acknowledge expertise**: "Some of you already work with this — I'd love to hear your experience in 5 minutes" before or after a concept
- **Real-brand application**: Always apply frameworks to a brand students know (a global brand or a local familiar brand)
- **Peer-to-peer learning**: Build in structured sharing rounds (2-minute pair shares after each concept)
- **Autonomy in exercises**: Give choice of industry/brand/scenario within exercises
- **Immediate applicability**: At end of each exercise, ask: "How would you use this at your company?"

## 2. Eight-Hour Session Structure

### Core Scheduling Rules

Based on Austin Govella's research on workshop scheduling:

1. **Start at 9:00 AM, not 8:00 AM** — Participants need mental settling time
2. **No session block longer than 3 hours** — Cognitive capacity depletes beyond this
3. **Two main 3-hour sessions** — Morning (9:00–12:00) and afternoon (13:00–16:00)
4. **Buffer the end** — End at 16:30–17:00 to allow wrap-up, reflection, and informal discussion

**Source**: [AGUX — Five Rules for Scheduling Workshops](https://www.agux.co/workshops/planning/five-rules-for-scheduling-workshops)

### Recommended 8-Hour Day Structure

```
09:00 – 09:20  Welcome, framing, agenda overview (20 min)
09:20 – 09:50  Icebreaker / warm-up exercise (30 min)
09:50 – 10:30  LECTURE BLOCK 1: Digital Marketing Landscape + SOSTAC (40 min)
10:30 – 11:00  WORKSHOP 1: SOSTAC situation audit on a live brand (30 min)
11:00 – 11:15  BREAK (15 min)
11:15 – 11:45  LECTURE BLOCK 2: RACE Framework + customer journey (30 min)
11:45 – 12:30  WORKSHOP 2: RACE mapping exercise (45 min)
12:30 – 13:30  LUNCH (60 min)
13:30 – 13:50  Re-energizer / afternoon warm-up (20 min)
13:50 – 14:20  LECTURE BLOCK 3: Brand strategy + CVP (30 min)
14:20 – 15:10  WORKSHOP 3: CVP Canvas exercise (50 min)
15:10 – 15:25  BREAK (15 min)
15:25 – 15:50  LECTURE BLOCK 4: Digital channel mix + KPIs (25 min)
15:50 – 16:30  WORKSHOP 4: Channel planning exercise (40 min)
16:30 – 17:00  Synthesis, reflection, takeaways, Q&A (30 min)
```

**Total time**: 8 hours
- Direct instruction: ~2 hours 5 minutes
- Workshop exercises: ~2 hours 45 minutes
- Transitions + facilitation: ~40 minutes
- Breaks + lunch: ~1 hour 30 minutes

### Cognitive Load and Break Timing

The **ultradian rhythm** (Nathaniel Kleitman's research) shows humans operate in natural 90–120 minute cycles of high-then-low alertness. For workshop design:

- Schedule a break **every 75–90 minutes** at minimum
- Breaks of **15 minutes** maintain momentum without losing the group's energy
- Lunch break of **60 minutes** allows real mental recovery
- After lunch, schedule a **low-cognitive warm-up activity** before re-entering content (not a heavy lecture block)

Research on Pomodoro-style breaks showed that structured breaks reduce fatigue, improve focus, and increase performance scores compared to unstructured long sessions.

**Source**: [PMC — Assessing the efficacy of the Pomodoro technique in enhancing anatomy lesson retention](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12532815/)

### Morning vs. Afternoon Activity Planning

| Time of Day | Energy Level | Best Activity Type |
|---|---|---|
| Morning (9:00–12:00) | High alertness | Complex analysis, framework introduction, structured problem-solving |
| Post-lunch (13:00–14:00) | Energy dip | Light re-energizer, pair discussion, low-stakes brainstorm |
| Mid-afternoon (14:00–15:30) | Re-energized | Creative exercises, canvas work, collaborative mapping |
| Late afternoon (15:30–17:00) | Declining | Synthesis, presentations, channel planning (structured, concrete) |

**Rule**: Put cognitively demanding lecture-heavy content in the morning. Put collaborative, doing-based exercises in the afternoon. Never front-load the afternoon slot with a 45-minute lecture block.

## 3. Transitions Between Lecture and Workshop Modes

### The Transition Problem

When participants sit through 40 minutes of theory and are then asked to "now do this exercise," there is a cognitive gear-change required. Poor transitions cause hesitation, confusion, and disengagement.

### Signaling Mode Changes — Practical Techniques

**1. Verbal cues**
- Use a consistent phrase to mark the shift: "Theory off. Practice on."
- Announce clearly: "In the next 30 minutes, you are going to apply what we just covered to [Brand X]."

**2. Slide cues (Keynote)**
- Create a dedicated "WORKSHOP TIME" title slide with a distinct visual style (different background color, large heading, timer countdown)
- Use a consistent slide template for exercise instruction slides — students learn to recognize them
- The exercise instruction slide should show: Task | Time | Output | Tools

**3. Physical cues**
- Ask participants to move to their group tables (if possible)
- Put on background music during exercise time (no music during lecture)
- Set a visible timer on screen

**4. The "orient-before-you-open" rule**
- Before opening an exercise, spend 2–3 minutes demonstrating what the output looks like ("a filled canvas should look like this")
- Show a worked example first, even partially filled
- Then start the timer

**Source**: [SessionLab — How to improve the learning experience with workshop transitions](https://www.sessionlab.com/blog/workshop-transitions/); [Wavetable — How to design a workshop slide deck](https://www.wavetable.net/resources/how-to-design-a-workshop-slide-deck)

### Wonder and Wander Transition (Post-Lecture Re-set)

For transitions after intense lecture blocks (especially mid-morning or post-lunch), use a 5–10 minute transitional activity:
- Ask participants to write one question they now have on a sticky note
- Ask participants to share one connection between the theory just covered and something from their own work experience
- These both close the lecture and open the workshop mentally

## 4. Workshop Exercise Design for Digital Marketing

### Exercise Types That Work for Mixed-Experience Groups

When participants have mixed levels of experience (some practitioners, some theorists):

| Problem | Solution |
|---|---|
| Advanced participants dominate | Anonymous digital sticky notes before verbal sharing |
| Beginners feel lost | Provide a partially filled example/template before starting |
| Groupthink in teams | Start every exercise solo (3–5 min individual think) before group work |
| Energy drops | Inject a 2-minute "share with your neighbor" micro-break mid-exercise |

**Facilitation principle**: Combine **"together alone"** time (each person works in parallel in silence) with **collaborative synthesis** time. Never start a group exercise by saying "discuss as a group" — begin individual, then merge.

**Source**: [The Facilitator — Marketing Strategy Workshop](https://www.facilitator.com/blog/marketing-strategy-workshop)

---

### Exercise 1: SOSTAC Situation Audit (Workshop 1)

**Duration**: 30–45 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Tools**: Canva Whiteboard (SOSTAC template), sticky notes

**Structure**:
1. (5 min) Facilitator assigns or groups choose a brand (FMCG, tech, fashion, or a local brand)
2. (3 min) Facilitator shows a partially filled SOSTAC example on the slide
3. (15 min) TOGETHER ALONE — each person adds sticky notes to the SOSTAC board for their assigned section (Situation, Objectives, Strategy, Tactics, Action, Control)
4. (7 min) Group consolidates: duplicates removed, gaps identified, 1 insight per section highlighted
5. (5 min) One representative shares 3 findings; group votes with dot on most interesting insight

**Canva Whiteboard setup**:
- Create a SOSTAC grid (6 columns or 6 sections on infinite canvas)
- Pre-assign sticky note colors per section (e.g., blue = Situation, green = Objectives)
- Use the built-in timer (set to 15 min for solo phase, 7 min for group phase)

**Facilitator guidance**:
- Walk the room during solo phase — ensure everyone is adding notes, not waiting
- If a group is stuck on "Control," prompt: "What metrics would you measure? How often?"

---

### Exercise 2: RACE Customer Journey Mapping (Workshop 2)

**Duration**: 45 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Tools**: Canva Whiteboard (customer journey map template), sticky notes

**Structure**:
1. (5 min) Facilitator explains: RACE = Reach, Act, Convert, Engage — and shows a real brand journey example
2. (5 min) Groups agree on their chosen brand and a specific customer persona (age, goal, starting point)
3. (20 min) Map the customer journey across the four RACE stages:
   - Reach: How does the customer first discover this brand?
   - Act: What actions do they take before deciding?
   - Convert: What converts them? What friction exists?
   - Engage: What brings them back? What builds loyalty?
4. (10 min) Groups annotate with "what's missing / what could be improved" in a different color
5. (5 min) Gallery walk — each group briefly explains one key insight to another group

**Canva Whiteboard setup**:
- Horizontal swim lanes (one per RACE stage)
- Different sticky note color per stage
- Add emoji reactions for "pain points" vs "delight moments"
- Timer: 20 min for mapping, 5 min for annotation

**Source**: [Smart Insights — RACE Digital Marketing Planning Framework](https://www.smartinsights.com/digital-marketing-strategy/race-a-practical-framework-to-improve-your-digital-marketing/)

---

### Exercise 3: Value Proposition Canvas (Workshop 3)

**Duration**: 50 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Tools**: Canva Whiteboard (Value Proposition Canvas template)

**Structure** (adapted from Strategyzer method):
1. (5 min) Show the VPC model: Customer Profile (Jobs, Pains, Gains) + Value Map (Products, Pain Relievers, Gain Creators)
2. (5 min) Groups pick a customer segment for their chosen brand
3. (10 min) TOGETHER ALONE — fill Customer Profile section (sticky notes per category)
4. (10 min) Group consolidates Customer Profile — rank top 3 pains and top 3 gains
5. (10 min) TOGETHER ALONE — fill Value Map section (how does the brand address those pains/gains?)
6. (5 min) Group identifies: which Pain Relievers and Gain Creators are missing from the brand's current offering?
7. (5 min) Share one gap or opportunity with the room

**Canva Whiteboard setup**:
- Use the standard VPC canvas template (circle + square)
- Assign different colored sticky notes for Jobs, Pains, Gains (customer side) and Products, Pain Relievers, Gain Creators (value side)
- Optional: use voting stickers to highlight the most important items after group consolidation

**Recommended timing** (from Learning Loop research): Full VPC workshop needs 60–90 min; condensed academic version works in 50 min if customer profile preparation is scaffolded.

**Source**: [Learning Loop — Value Proposition Mapping](https://learningloop.io/plays/workshop-exercise/value-proposition-mapping)

---

### Exercise 4: Brand Positioning Perceptual Map (Workshop 3 alternative / add-on)

**Duration**: 25–30 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Tools**: Canva Whiteboard (two-axis map/X-Y grid), sticky notes

**Structure**:
1. (5 min) Groups choose a market category and identify 6–8 competing brands
2. (5 min) Agree on two key positioning axes (e.g., Price: Low–High vs. Quality: Basic–Premium; or Digital: Low–High vs. Age focus: Young–Mature)
3. (10 min) Place brands as sticky notes on the map — discuss placement as a group
4. (5 min) Identify the white space (empty map areas) — what does it represent? Is there an opportunity?
5. (5 min) One group shares their map + white space insight

**Source**: [Great Ideas for Teaching Marketing — Perceptual Map](https://www.greatideasforteachingmarketing.com/creating-and-interpreting-a-perceptual-map/); [Perceptual Maps — Market Positioning](https://www.learnmarketing.net/perceptualmaps.htm)

---

### Exercise 5: Digital Channel Mix Planning (Workshop 4)

**Duration**: 40 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Tools**: Canva Whiteboard (table/matrix template), sticky notes or text boxes

**Structure**:
1. (5 min) Facilitator presents a blank channel planning matrix:
   - Rows: channels (SEO, Social, Email, Paid, Content, Influencer, etc.)
   - Columns: RACE stage (Reach / Act / Convert / Engage)
   - Plus columns: Priority (High/Med/Low) and Metric
2. (5 min) Groups review their brand and its current apparent channel use
3. (20 min) Fill the matrix: which channels serve which RACE stages? What KPIs would you track?
4. (5 min) Identify: which RACE stage is under-served? What one channel addition would you recommend?
5. (5 min) Recommendations shared across groups — facilitator captures patterns

**Source**: [Smart Insights — SOSTAC + RACE combined planning](https://www.smartinsights.com/digital-marketing-strategy/sostac-plan-example/)

## 5. Canva Whiteboard — Facilitation Guide

### Core Features for Workshop Facilitators

| Feature | How to Use in a Workshop |
|---|---|
| **Infinite canvas** | Create separate zones for each exercise; groups work in their own zone |
| **Sticky notes** | Core brainstorming tool; color-code by category or group |
| **Sticky note sorting** | After brainstorm, select notes and use Sort to group by theme |
| **AI Summary** | At end of brainstorm, generate summary from sticky notes to capture insights |
| **Timer** | Set countdown visible to all — critical for time-boxed individual work phases |
| **Voting stickers / reactions** | Dot voting to prioritize ideas; use after sticky note brainstorms |
| **Draw tool** | For annotating maps, drawing arrows, sketching positioning maps |
| **Smart shapes** | For creating clean frameworks (2x2 matrices, canvases) |
| **Real-time cursors** | All participants see each other's movements — encourages engagement |
| **Expand to Whiteboard** | Transition seamlessly from a Keynote/presentation view into a whiteboard |
| **Templates** | Pre-built frameworks for strategy mapping, customer journeys, design thinking |

**Sources**: [Canva — Online Whiteboard](https://www.canva.com/online-whiteboard/); [DM Letter Studio — Canva Whiteboard Features](https://dmletterstudio.com/canva-whiteboard-features/); [Innovation Training — Canva Whiteboard Templates](https://www.innovationtraining.org/canva-whiteboard-templates/)

### Best Templates for Marketing Workshops

1. **Customer Journey Map** — Horizontal swim lanes per RACE stage; spaces for touchpoints, emotions, pain points
2. **SWOT Analysis** — 4-quadrant board; use as situation audit anchor
3. **Brainstorm Mind Map** — Spider diagram for generating initial ideas about a brand
4. **Stop/Start/Continue Retrospective** — Useful for wrap-up reflection on strategy
5. **Design Thinking Ideation** — Diverge (ideas) then converge (voting) — directly applicable to campaign ideation
6. **Idea Book (sticky note format)** — Quick-capture brainstorm for any open question

### Setting Up Canva Whiteboard for a Workshop

**Before the session**:
1. Create the whiteboard with clearly labeled zones: one per group (Group A, Group B, Group C, Group D)
2. Within each group zone, place the exercise template (pre-built or customized)
3. Add sticky notes in the right colors already on the board — students just add text, not configure colors
4. Pre-fill 1–2 example sticky notes per section to model the expected format
5. Test the timer feature
6. Prepare a share link (view + edit access) — share via QR code on the slide

**During the session**:
1. Walk participants through the whiteboard with a 2-minute tour before the exercise starts
2. Launch the timer for each phase (solo work, group work)
3. Use the "Summon" feature (if available) to pull all participants to a specific area for group debrief
4. Keep one "Facilitation Zone" on the canvas to add notes, emerging themes, and cross-group insights
5. Capture screenshots at key moments

**After the session**:
1. Export whiteboard as PDF or image for sharing with all participants
2. Use AI Summary feature to extract key insights from sticky note clusters
3. Share export within 24 hours while content is fresh

### Facilitation Tips for Digital Whiteboards

- **Start with a warm-up sticky note** in the first 5 minutes — "Add your name + one brand you admire in our whiteboard." This orients everyone to the tool before the real exercise.
- **Silent solo phase first** — Use the timer to enforce 5–10 minutes of individual sticky note posting before any discussion
- **Prevent groupthink** — Anonymous sticky note mode (remove names) or "together alone" parallel work
- **Provide explicit instructions on the canvas itself** — Write exercise instructions as text on the board, not just on slides. Participants should be able to see both simultaneously.
- **Debrief on screen** — After each exercise, zoom into each group's zone as they present, showing the whole class what they built
- **Manage digital noise** — Warn participants to mute notifications; remind them the whiteboard is the only tab they need open

## 6. Keynote Presentation Best Practices

### Slide Types for a Lecture-Workshop Hybrid Day

Create four distinct slide types with visually distinct styling:

| Slide Type | Visual Style | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| **Concept slides** | Clean, 1 key idea, supporting visual | Explain a framework or principle |
| **Workshop instruction slides** | High contrast background (distinct color), clear task format | Signal exercise mode |
| **Example slides** | Shows a filled-in template or worked example | Model expected output before exercise |
| **Agenda/transition slides** | Simple, text-dominant, progress indicator | Orient participants in the day |

### Slide Count Recommendations for 8 Hours

**Target**: Approximately 80–120 slides total for an 8-hour day

Breakdown by block (approximate):
- Welcome + framing: 8–10 slides
- Lecture Block 1 (SOSTAC): 12–15 slides
- Workshop 1 instruction + example: 4–5 slides
- Lecture Block 2 (RACE): 10–12 slides
- Workshop 2 instruction + example: 4–5 slides
- Lecture Block 3 (Brand/CVP): 10–12 slides
- Workshop 3 instruction + example: 5–6 slides
- Lecture Block 4 (Channels/KPIs): 8–10 slides
- Workshop 4 instruction + example: 4–5 slides
- Wrap-up + reflection: 5–7 slides

**Rule of thumb**: 1 slide per 2–3 minutes of lecture; workshop slides are sparse (1 instruction slide + 1 example slide per exercise).

**Source**: [VisualHackers — 17 Presentation Techniques For A Keynote](https://visualhackers.com/blog/17-presentation-techniques-for-a-great-keynote/)

### Visual Design Principles for Educational Presentations

- **Maximum 3 key points per slide** — Never more than one concept idea per slide
- **Minimum 30pt font** — For any text meant to be read from a screen
- **Prefer visuals over bullet points** — Diagrams, icons, frameworks, annotated screenshots
- **7±2 principle** — Adults retain 5–9 pieces of information simultaneously; design slides to stay within this
- **Consistent color coding** — Match slide colors to framework elements (e.g., RACE stages always in the same colors throughout the deck)
- **Full-bleed images as context** — Background images that support the concept without requiring explanation
- **White space is your friend** — Busy slides compete with the facilitator's voice

**Source**: [Wavetable — How to design a workshop slide deck](https://www.wavetable.net/resources/how-to-design-a-workshop-slide-deck); [VisualHackers — 17 Keynote Techniques](https://visualhackers.com/blog/17-presentation-techniques-for-a-great-keynote/)

### Workshop Instruction Slide Template

Every exercise transition slide should contain exactly these five elements:

```
[WORKSHOP: Exercise Name]          <- Large heading on distinct background

TASK:
In your groups, [clear one-sentence task description]

TIME: [X] minutes

OUTPUT:
A completed [canvas/map/matrix] with [specific deliverable]

TOOLS:
Canva Whiteboard — [zone name/link]
```

Add a QR code or short URL to the Canva board on every exercise instruction slide.

### Presenter Notes Best Practices

Keynote presenter notes are your facilitation script. Use them to:

- **Concept slides**: Write the 2–3 sentences to say aloud (not bullet points to read — the script itself)
- **Transition points**: Write explicit cue: "PAUSE HERE — ask if there are questions before moving to exercise"
- **Exercise slides**: Write what to watch for during the exercise, common problems, and what to say when debriefing
- **Time cues**: Note your target time at the bottom of the notes for each slide: "(Target: 10:45)"
- **Discussion prompts**: Write 2–3 questions to ask if the group finishes early or if you need to extend a discussion

**Format suggestion** for presenter notes:
```
[SAY]: The SOSTAC framework was developed by PR Smith in the 1990s...
[DO]: Click to animate the six stages one at a time
[WATCH]: Check if participants are familiar — ask "who has used this before?"
[TIME]: Should reach this slide by 10:00am
```

### Signaling Lecture-to-Workshop Mode Changes in Keynote

1. **Dedicated transition slide** — "Now it's your turn" slide with a distinct visual language (different color, workshop icon)
2. **Timer slide** — Use Keynote's built-in live clock or a countdown slide; or embed a countdown from a web timer using a browser in presentation mode
3. **Consistent ordering** — Every exercise follows the same 3-slide sequence: (1) Instruction slide, (2) Example output slide, (3) Timer/active slide. Students learn the rhythm.
4. **Return slide** — After exercise, a "Welcome back" slide that opens the debrief. Visually distinct from both lecture and exercise slides.

## 7. Icebreaker and Re-energizer Activities

### Opening Warm-Up (30 minutes, 09:20–09:50)

**Activity: "Brand You Trust + Brand You Don't"**
- Participants add 2 sticky notes to Canva Whiteboard: one brand they trust, one they distrust
- Solo: 5 minutes (everyone adds notes simultaneously)
- Group: Cluster similar responses, identify patterns: 10 minutes
- Debrief: Facilitator highlights patterns and bridges to "what makes great digital marketing?" — 10 minutes
- Purpose: Activates prior knowledge, warms up Canva Whiteboard use, surfaces relevant experience

### Post-Lunch Re-Energizer (20 minutes, 13:30–13:50)

**Activity: "Digital Campaign Quickfire"**
- Show 5 real digital campaign screenshots (30 seconds each): social ads, email, landing pages, influencer post, SEO listing
- Participants call out: Good or Bad? Why?
- Low stakes, opinion-based, fast-paced
- Purpose: Re-activates critical marketing thinking without requiring cognitive load; wakes up the group after lunch

**Alternative**: "Two Truths and a Lie — Marketing Edition"
- Each group creates 3 statements about a brand's digital strategy (2 true, 1 false)
- Other groups guess the lie
- 15 minutes total; high energy, social

## 8. Handling a Mixed-Experience Group

### The Core Challenge

Postgraduate cohorts for digital marketing typically contain:
- Practitioners (2–10 years in marketing) — may disengage during basics
- Career changers — may be overwhelmed by jargon
- International students — may have culturally different brand references

### Strategies

**For practitioners in the group**:
- Assign them the role of "skeptic" or "devil's advocate" in group exercises
- Ask them to share a real-world example before you explain the framework
- Offer extension questions on exercise instruction slides ("Advanced: How would this change for a B2B brand?")

**For newcomers**:
- Pre-fill the first section of every canvas template as an example
- Use globally known brands (Apple, Nike, Netflix) for worked examples so everyone has a reference point
- Pair newcomers with practitioners in group assignments

**For international diversity**:
- Allow groups to choose brands from their own country for exercises
- Use visual frameworks (canvases, maps) rather than text-heavy deliverables — reduces language barrier
- Run anonymous sticky note rounds before verbal sharing

**Source**: [The Facilitator — Marketing Strategy Workshop facilitation tips](https://www.facilitator.com/blog/marketing-strategy-workshop); [Brand Credential — 10 Creative Marketing Workshop Ideas](https://www.brandcredential.com/post/10-creative-ideas-for-a-marketing-strategy-workshop)

## 9. Digital Channel Mix Exercise Detail

**Exercise: "Marketing Budget Challenge" (40 minutes)**

Structure:
1. (5 min) Each group receives a hypothetical marketing budget: €50,000 for a 6-month digital campaign
2. (3 min) Groups define their brand's primary customer persona and main business objective
3. (20 min) Allocate the budget across channels on the Channel Matrix whiteboard:
   - Paid social (Meta/LinkedIn/TikTok)
   - SEO + content
   - Email marketing
   - Paid search (Google Ads)
   - Influencer/partnership
   - Website CRO
   For each channel: allocation (€), RACE stage served, primary KPI
4. (7 min) Groups present their rationale — 1 minute per group
5. (5 min) Facilitator highlights divergence across groups and why different choices are valid

**Why this works for mixed groups**: Budget constraints force explicit prioritization decisions. There is no single right answer. Practitioners will recognize real-world trade-offs; newcomers will learn the trade-offs through discussion.

**Source**: [Brand Credential — Marketing Budget Challenge exercise](https://www.brandcredential.com/post/10-creative-ideas-for-a-marketing-strategy-workshop)

## 10. Summary Checklist for Facilitators

### Before the Day

- [ ] All Canva Whiteboard zones pre-built with templates, example sticky notes, and color coding
- [ ] Share link ready (QR code on slide)
- [ ] All exercise instruction slides have: Task, Time, Output, Tools
- [ ] Keynote presenter notes include timing cues and debrief questions
- [ ] Post-lunch re-energizer prepared (campaign images or quiz)
- [ ] Extension questions prepared for advanced participants
- [ ] Worked examples ready for each canvas/exercise

### During the Day

- [ ] Start with warm-up on Canva Whiteboard — every participant adds at least one sticky note in the first 15 minutes
- [ ] Use the timer feature for every exercise phase
- [ ] Solo phase first, group phase second — never open with "discuss"
- [ ] Background music during exercise time
- [ ] Walk the room during exercises — spot groups that are stuck
- [ ] Keep a facilitation zone on the whiteboard to note emerging themes
- [ ] Take a whiteboard screenshot after each exercise

### After the Day

- [ ] Export whiteboard and share with all participants (within 24 hours)
- [ ] Send a one-page summary of key frameworks covered + exercise outputs
- [ ] Invite 3-sentence written reflection: What will you apply first?
