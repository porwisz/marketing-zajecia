# Digital Marketing Strategy: Frameworks, Models & Theories
## Research Findings — Postgraduate Course Design

**Research Question**: What are the key frameworks, models, and theories needed to teach Digital Marketing Strategy in a postgraduate course?

**Sources gathered**: prsmith.org, davechaffey.com, strategyzer.com, shopify.com, techtarget.com, indeed.com, kaushik.net, and additional academic/industry references.

---

## 1. What Is Digital Strategy vs. Digital Marketing Strategy

### Digital Strategy (Broad — Organizational Level)

**Definition** (Dave Chaffey, davechaffey.com):
> "An organization's priority initiatives for future investment in digital technology to make a business more competitive by digitalization."

Digital strategy is organization-wide. It covers how digital technology transforms processes, business models, products, and customer interactions across the entire enterprise.

**Definition** (TechTarget):
> "A plan for maximizing the business benefits of data assets and technology-focused initiatives." Also known as "digital media strategy."

**Components of digital strategy**:
- Cross-functional leadership (executives, marketing, IT)
- Short-term actionable roadmaps tied to measurable business objectives
- Assessment of competitive positioning and implementation barriers
- Measurement tools for continuous improvement
- Culture change promoting data-driven decision-making

**Why it matters**: Organizations use digital strategies to identify vulnerable areas in value chains, create new business model opportunities, improve operational efficiency, and gain competitive advantages.

### Digital Marketing Strategy (Focused — Marketing Level)

**Definition** (Dave Chaffey, davechaffey.com):
> "Defines the focus of digital marketing efforts to improve the contribution digital makes to an organization."

Digital marketing strategy is a subset of digital strategy. It is documented within a digital marketing plan and shows how digital marketing initiatives connect with broader organizational activities.

**Key distinction**:

| Dimension | Digital Strategy | Digital Marketing Strategy |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------------|
| Scope | Entire organization | Marketing function |
| Focus | Technology investment, business transformation | Attracting, converting, retaining customers online |
| Owner | C-suite, CTO, CIO, CEO | CMO, Digital Marketing Director |
| Timeframe | Multi-year transformation | Campaign/annual planning cycle |
| Typical tools | Business Model Canvas, IT roadmaps | SOSTAC, RACE, SEO, social media, email |

**Important note for teaching**: An effective digital marketing strategy "should include how digital integrates with other activities" — it does not exist in isolation from the broader organizational digital strategy or from offline marketing.

## 2. SOSTAC® Model (PR Smith)

**Source**: prsmith.org — official SOSTAC® documentation

### Overview

SOSTAC® is a systematic planning methodology created by PR Smith. It is one of the most widely used marketing planning frameworks globally, covering six sequential stages that move from diagnosis to execution to monitoring.

**Full form**:
- **S** — Situation Analysis (Where are we now?)
- **O** — Objectives (Where do we want to be?)
- **S** — Strategy (How do we get there — at a high level?)
- **T** — Tactics (What specific tools and channels do we use?)
- **A** — Actions (Who does what, by when, with what budget?)
- **C** — Control (How do we measure progress and adjust?)

### Component-by-Component Breakdown

#### S — Situation Analysis
The foundation stage. Typically represents **half the planning effort**, with detailed findings in appendices and key insights in the executive summary.

Four critical areas to examine:
1. **Customer Analysis** — "Who? Why? And How?" about target audiences
2. **Competitor Analysis** — direct competitors and new market entrants
3. **Company Analysis** — internal strengths, weaknesses, past performance
4. **External Analysis** — uncontrollable PEST/PESTEL factors (Political, Economic, Social, Technical, Environmental, Legal)

**Digital application**: Digital Situation Analysis adds website analytics (traffic, conversion rates, bounce rates), social media audits, SEO performance, digital customer personas, and competitor digital benchmarking.

#### O — Objectives
Span from organizational vision down to specific KPIs.

Typical objectives:
- Sales and profit/ROI targets
- Market share and conversion rates
- Cost per acquisition (CPA) and customer retention metrics
- Brand awareness and preference goals

**All objectives must be SMART**: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-bound.

**Digital application**: Digital objectives commonly use the "5 Ds of Digital" (Digital Devices, Digital Platforms, Digital Media, Digital Data, Digital Technology) and target metrics like website traffic growth, leads generated, email subscribers, social media reach, and e-commerce conversion rates.

#### S — Strategy
The shortest but most critical section. Provides directional clarity for all subsequent tactics.

PR Smith uses the **"TOPPP SEED"** acronym for strategy variables:
- **T** — Target Markets and Positioning
- **O** — Objectives and Partnerships
- **P** — Processes (including AI integration)
- **P** — Stages/Sequence
- **S** — Engagement
- **E** — Experience
- **E** — Data

Strategic decisions remove barriers to achieving objectives and establish sustainable competitive advantage.

**Digital application**: Strategy decisions include whether to prioritize SEO vs. paid search, which social platforms to focus on, whether to use influencer marketing, how to segment and personalize, and which customer acquisition channels to prioritize.

#### T — Tactics
The operational marketing mix — the specific digital channels and tools used to execute the strategy.

Includes:
- Pricing and product/service adjustments
- Channel selection for customer journeys
- Personalized automated communications
- "Always-On" campaigns adjusted via real-time data feedback
- Content marketing, SEO, social media, email, PPC, retargeting, etc.

**Digital application**: Tactics in digital marketing correspond to the RACE stages (see Section 3): which digital touchpoints to use at each stage of the customer journey.

#### A — Actions
Ensures passionate, excellent implementation through:
- Internal marketing and team motivation
- Clear assignment of responsibilities (who, what, when, how much)
- Skill-set development and resource allocation
- Project management for individual tactical channels

**The 4Ms of Effective Action**:
- **Men and women** — Human resources and talent
- **Money** — Budget allocation
- **Minutes** — Timescales and schedules
- **Megadata** — Analytics and insights

#### C — Control
Establishes the measurement framework:
- What metrics to measure (KPIs per channel and stage)
- Measurement frequency and costs
- Response protocols for performance deviations
- Feedback loops informing future planning cycles

**Agile approach**: Creates 90-day review cycles, forming a mini-loop between Situation–Objectives–Control that enables responsive adjustment.

**Digital application**: Digital control uses analytics dashboards (Google Analytics, CRM data), A/B testing results, campaign performance reports, and attribution modelling.

### How SOSTAC Differs from Traditional Marketing Planning

Traditional marketing planning often follows SWOT → Mix → Budget → Timeline sequentially without tight feedback loops. SOSTAC adds:

1. **Integrated measurement**: Control closes the loop back to Situation, enabling agile planning
2. **Digital-native readiness**: AI, data analytics, and digital channel integration built into Tactics and Actions
3. **90-day sprint structure**: Enables continuous adaptation rather than annual static plans
4. **Clear strategic layer**: Traditional plans often jump from SWOT directly to tactics; SOSTAC inserts a distinct Strategy stage

### SOSTAC in a Workshop Setting

The SOSTAC framework adapts well to workshop exercises:

**Suggested workshop structure (3-hour format)**:
1. **Situation Audit** (45 min): Groups perform a rapid PESTEL + competitor digital audit on a real or case-study brand
2. **SMART Objectives** (20 min): Set 3–5 specific digital marketing objectives for a 12-month period
3. **Strategy Brief** (30 min): One-page strategy statement defining target audiences, positioning, and key channels
4. **Tactics Map** (30 min): Map specific digital tactics to each stage of the customer journey (using RACE overlay)
5. **Actions Plan** (20 min): Assign owners, timelines, and budgets to the top 3 tactics
6. **Control Dashboard** (15 min): Identify 5–7 KPIs and define measurement cadence

SOSTAC can serve as the backbone for a full semester-long course project, where students develop a complete digital marketing plan for a real or hypothetical brand.

## 3. RACE Framework (Dave Chaffey / Smart Insights)

**Source**: davechaffey.com — official RACE model glossary entry

### Overview

The RACE Planning Framework was developed by Dr. Dave Chaffey in 2010 to help businesses create integrated marketing plans across online and offline channels. It maps the customer lifecycle from initial awareness through to long-term loyalty.

**Full form**:
- **R** — Reach
- **A** — Act
- **C** — Convert
- **E** — Engage

### Stage-by-Stage Breakdown

#### R — Reach
**Definition**: Building brand awareness and visibility to drive traffic across owned, paid, and earned media.

> "Reach is aimed at building awareness and visibility of your brand, products and services."

**Channels**: Search (SEO and PPC), social media, content marketing, display advertising, PR, influencer marketing, video (YouTube), podcasts.

**Key KPIs**:
- Unique website visitors / sessions
- Impressions and organic reach
- Share of voice
- Follower growth (social media)
- Keyword rankings (SEO)
- Click-through rate (CTR) on ads
- Cost per click (CPC)

**Customer journey position**: Top of funnel (TOFU) — Awareness and discovery.

#### A — Act (Interact)
**Definition**: Encouraging customer interactions and generating leads. Focuses on persuading visitors to take meaningful actions that move them along the customer journey.

Examples of Act-stage goals: reading a blog post, viewing a product, signing up for a newsletter, downloading a lead magnet, requesting a demo.

**Key KPIs**:
- Pages per session / time on site
- Bounce rate (lower = better engagement)
- Email newsletter sign-ups
- Lead form completions
- Product page views
- "Added to basket" events
- Content downloads / whitepaper downloads

**Customer journey position**: Middle of funnel (MOFU) — Consideration and interest.

#### C — Convert
**Definition**: Completing the sale through online or offline channels. Acknowledges that "conversion to sale, either in online and/or offline channels" is the critical objective — many transactions still occur offline even when the journey started online.

**Key KPIs**:
- Conversion rate (% of visitors who purchase)
- Revenue / Average Order Value (AOV)
- Cost per acquisition (CPA)
- Online vs. offline conversion attribution
- Abandoned cart recovery rate
- Lead-to-customer conversion rate (B2B)

**Customer journey position**: Bottom of funnel (BOFU) — Decision and purchase.

#### E — Engage
**Definition**: Building long-term customer loyalty through continued communication and repeat interactions.

**Key metrics**: Repeat purchases, content sharing, customer satisfaction scores (NPS/CSAT), lifetime value (LTV), advocacy.

**Key KPIs**:
- Customer Lifetime Value (CLV/LTV)
- Net Promoter Score (NPS)
- Repeat purchase rate
- Email open rates / click rates (retention campaigns)
- Social media shares and mentions (advocacy)
- Churn rate (inverse)
- Customer satisfaction score (CSAT)

**Customer journey position**: Post-purchase — Loyalty and advocacy.

### RACE as a Customer Journey Framework

RACE follows "the established customer lifecycle of relationship building or marketing funnel from creating awareness; generating leads from new prospects; converting prospects to sale online or offline and encouraging loyalty, repeat sales and advocacy."

It maps directly to traditional funnel stages:

```
RACE Stage    |  Funnel Position  |  Customer State
-----------   | ----------------  | ---------------
Reach         |  Awareness        |  "I've heard of this brand"
Act           |  Consideration    |  "I'm interested and exploring"
Convert       |  Decision         |  "I'm ready to buy"
Engage        |  Loyalty          |  "I'm a repeat customer and advocate"
```

### RACE and SOSTAC Integration

RACE and SOSTAC are designed as complementary frameworks (Dr. Dave Chaffey, davechaffey.com confirms this):

- **SOSTAC** provides the overall strategic planning process (the "how to plan")
- **RACE** provides the customer-facing activity framework (the "what activities to execute")

Integration mapping:
- SOSTAC's **Situation** → audit current performance across all RACE stages
- SOSTAC's **Objectives** → set KPI targets for each RACE stage
- SOSTAC's **Strategy** → determine which RACE stages to prioritize and how to differentiate at each
- SOSTAC's **Tactics** → specific digital channels and content for each RACE stage
- SOSTAC's **Actions** → execution plans for RACE-stage campaigns
- SOSTAC's **Control** → dashboards tracking KPIs for each RACE stage

**Teaching insight**: Using SOSTAC + RACE together gives students both the planning structure and the execution framework — they are the two most commonly used frameworks in UK digital marketing practice and education.

## 4. Customer Value Proposition (CVP)

### 4.1 Definition

A **Customer Value Proposition (CVP)** is a concise statement that communicates the unique benefits a product or service delivers to a specific target customer, explaining why that customer should choose this offering over alternatives.

**From Shopify**:
> "A value proposition ranges from a single headline to a short paragraph that highlights the specific problem you solve and why you're superior to competitors."

### 4.2 CVP vs. USP — Key Distinction

| | Customer Value Proposition | Unique Selling Proposition (USP) |
|--|---------------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Scope** | Broader — all the ways the product improves a customer's life | Narrower — the ONE special thing you do that competitors can't |
| **Focus** | Customer benefits and outcomes | Product differentiator |
| **Audience** | Customer-centric (what's in it for me?) | Market-positioning (why us vs. them?) |
| **Length** | Can be a headline + subhead + 3 bullet points | Often a single sentence or tagline |
| **Example** | "Spotify helps you discover music you love, listen anywhere, and share with friends — free or premium" | "The world's largest music streaming platform" |

**Rule of thumb**: USP answers "What makes us different?" — CVP answers "Why should the customer care?"

### 4.3 Components of a Strong CVP (Three Core Elements)

1. **Specificity** — Clear, concrete benefits understood within seconds by the target customer
2. **Pain Point Focus** — Directly addresses a customer problem or desired outcome
3. **Exclusivity / Competitive Superiority** — Demonstrates why your solution beats alternatives

### 4.4 The Value Proposition Canvas (Osterwalder / Strategyzer)

Developed by Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur (authors of "Business Model Generation"), the Value Proposition Canvas is a visual tool for systematically designing a CVP.

**Source**: strategyzer.com

#### The Canvas Has Two Sides:

**Side 1 — Customer Profile** (right circle)
Maps what the customer cares about:
- **Customer Jobs** — What tasks is the customer trying to accomplish? (functional, social, emotional jobs)
- **Customer Pains** — What frustrations, risks, or obstacles do they encounter?
- **Customer Gains** — What outcomes or benefits do they want to achieve?

**Side 2 — Value Map** (left square)
Maps what your offering does:
- **Products & Services** — The core product/service you offer
- **Pain Relievers** — How your offering alleviates specific customer pains
- **Gain Creators** — How your offering creates desired gains for the customer

#### The "Fit" Principle
The canvas achieves **product-market fit** when the Pain Relievers and Gain Creators on your Value Map directly address the most important Pains and Gains in the Customer Profile.

**Workshop application**: Students map a digital brand (e.g., Spotify, Airbnb, Deliveroo) using the canvas, starting with the Customer Profile (based on persona research) before defining the Value Map — forcing customer-first thinking.

### 4.5 Steps to Build a CVP (Practical Process)

1. **Customer Research** — Identify target segment; interview or survey to uncover jobs, pains, and gains
2. **Competitor Mapping** — Analyse competitor value propositions; identify white space
3. **Draft 3–5 Variants** — Write multiple CVP options; test headlines with target audience
4. **A/B Testing** — Test CVP variants on landing pages, ads, or emails (digital advantage: rapid testing)
5. **Implement & Monitor** — Deploy winning CVP across all digital touchpoints (website hero, ads, social bios, email subject lines)
6. **Iterate** — Review biannually or when market conditions change

### 4.6 CVP Examples from Digital Brands

| Brand | CVP (approximate) | Key pain addressed |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **Airbnb** | "Belong anywhere — book unique homes & experiences, not just rooms" | Impersonal hotel experience; desire for local immersion |
| **Spotify** | "Music for everyone — free with ads or premium without" | Cost barrier to large music library; personalized discovery |
| **Dropbox** | "Keep life organized and work moving — all your files accessible everywhere" | Losing files; can't share or access remotely |
| **Slack** | "Make work life simpler, more pleasant, and more productive" | Email overload; fragmented team communication |
| **Netflix** | "Watch anywhere. Cancel anytime." | Commitment-phobia of subscriptions; device flexibility |

### 4.7 CVP in Digital Marketing Context

In digital marketing, the CVP should be:
- **Visible immediately** on the homepage hero section (above the fold)
- **Consistent** across all paid ads, landing pages, and email campaigns
- **Tested continuously** using A/B testing on digital platforms
- **Aligned with persona** — different segments may need different CVP angles

The CVP drives every digital marketing message: it is the foundation for ad copy, content strategy, social media tone, and SEO keyword strategy (what problems does your audience search for?).

## 5. Brand Strategy Fundamentals

### 5.1 What Is Brand Strategy?

**Definition** (from Indeed.com industry resources):
Brand strategy is "a tool that helps organizations better understand who they are and create a plan to communicate this to others." It shapes how customers perceive an organization and directly influences their emotions and behaviors.

A brand strategy defines **who the brand is** (identity, values, personality, purpose) rather than merely what it sells. It is long-term and relatively stable, even as marketing tactics change.

### 5.2 Brand Strategy vs. Marketing Strategy vs. Digital Strategy

| | Brand Strategy | Marketing Strategy | Digital Strategy |
|--|---------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Core question** | "Who are we and what do we stand for?" | "How do we attract and retain customers?" | "How do digital technologies make us more competitive?" |
| **Timeframe** | Long-term (5–10+ years) | Medium-term (1–3 years) | Medium to long-term |
| **Focus** | Identity, values, positioning, personality | Audience, channels, messaging, pricing | Technology, data, processes, digital channels |
| **Output** | Brand guidelines, tone of voice, visual identity | Campaign plans, content plans, channel strategy | Technology roadmap, digital transformation plan |
| **Owner** | Brand/Creative Director, CEO | CMO, Head of Marketing | CTO, CDO, CMO |
| **Example** | "We stand for sustainability, accessibility, and community" | "We'll use Instagram and email to acquire 25-34 year olds" | "We'll build a mobile app and data platform to personalize at scale" |

**Key insight for teaching**: Brand strategy is upstream of marketing strategy. You cannot build effective marketing campaigns without knowing your brand strategy — it determines tone, messaging, visual language, and which audiences resonate with your identity.

### 5.3 Key Components of Brand Strategy

#### Brand Purpose
Why does the brand exist beyond making money? (Simon Sinek's "Start With Why")
- Examples: Patagonia ("We're in business to save our home planet"), TOMS ("One for One")
- Digital implication: Purpose-driven brands generate organic advocacy; authenticity matters more online

#### Brand Positioning
Where does the brand sit in the competitive landscape in the mind of the target customer?

**Positioning Statement formula** (Geoffrey Moore adaptation):
> "For [target customer] who [has a specific need], [brand name] is a [market category] that [provides key benefit]. Unlike [key competitor], [brand name] [primary differentiator]."

**Digital implication**: Positioning informs SEO keyword strategy (what category does your brand own?), ad targeting (who sees your ads?), and content pillars.

#### Brand Promise
The single most important commitment the brand makes to customers. It is the core expectation customers can rely on every time.

Examples:
- **Amazon**: "Every day, we work to be the most customer-centric company on Earth"
- **Apple**: "Think Different" — premium, creative, design-led experience
- **IKEA**: "To offer a wide range of well-designed, functional home furnishing products at prices so low that as many people as possible can afford them"

**Digital implication**: Brand promise should be reflected in UX design, customer service response times, return policies, and post-purchase experience — all digital touchpoints.

#### Brand Identity
The visual and verbal system that expresses the brand:
- Logo, color palette, typography
- Tone of voice and copy style
- Photography and illustration style
- Motion/animation principles

**Digital implication**: Consistency across website, social media, email, display ads, and app is critical — and more visible/auditable than ever before.

#### Brand Architecture
How a company organizes its portfolio of brands:
1. **Monolithic/Branded House** — One master brand, all products under same name (Apple: iPhone, iPad, MacBook)
2. **House of Brands** — Independent brands, parent company in background (P&G: Ariel, Pampers, Gillette)
3. **Endorsed Brand** — Sub-brands endorsed by parent (Marriott: Courtyard by Marriott, Ritz-Carlton by Marriott)

**Digital implication**: Architecture decisions affect domain strategy, social media account structure, SEO, and app store presence.

### 5.4 Aaker Brand Equity Model

**Developed by**: David A. Aaker, Professor Emeritus at UC Berkeley Haas School of Business, in his 1991 book "Managing Brand Equity."

The Aaker model defines brand equity as the value added to a product or company by having a strong brand. It comprises five dimensions:

#### Dimension 1: Brand Loyalty
The degree to which customers repeatedly purchase from a brand and resist switching to competitors.
- Measured by: repeat purchase rate, churn rate, NPS
- Digital relevance: Email list engagement, subscription retention, loyalty programme participation

#### Dimension 2: Brand Awareness
The degree to which the brand is known and recognized in the target market.
Two levels:
- **Brand Recognition**: Customer can identify the brand when shown it
- **Brand Recall**: Customer can retrieve the brand from memory unaided
- Digital relevance: Organic search volume for brand name, direct traffic, social media followers and mentions

#### Dimension 3: Perceived Quality
The customer's overall perception of the quality of a brand's products or services, relative to alternatives.
- Not the same as actual quality — it's subjective perception
- Measured by: reviews, ratings, sentiment analysis
- Digital relevance: Google/Trustpilot reviews, App Store ratings, NPS scores, social listening

#### Dimension 4: Brand Associations
The mental connections, feelings, attributes, and images customers associate with the brand.
- Can be: functional (fast, cheap, secure), emotional (exciting, trustworthy), symbolic (status, belonging)
- Digital relevance: Social media listening reveals associations; content strategy shapes them; influencer partnerships transfer associations

#### Dimension 5: Other Proprietary Brand Assets
Patents, trademarks, channel relationships, and other unique competitive advantages.
- Digital relevance: Owned platform (e.g., Amazon's marketplace), data assets, app ecosystem, community

#### Aaker Model in Digital Context

| Aaker Dimension | Traditional Metric | Digital Metric |
|----------------|-------------------|----------------|
| Brand Loyalty | Repeat purchase, wallet share | CLV, churn rate, email engagement |
| Brand Awareness | Aided/unaided recall surveys | Branded search volume, direct traffic, social reach |
| Perceived Quality | Consumer surveys, focus groups | Review ratings, sentiment score, App Store rating |
| Brand Associations | Qualitative research | Social listening, keyword analysis, share of voice |
| Proprietary Assets | IP portfolio, distribution rights | Data assets, platform network effects, tech patents |

### 5.5 Brand Pyramid Framework

The Brand Pyramid (also called the Brand Essence Pyramid or Brand Key) organizes brand strategy from foundation to peak:

```
           [Brand Essence / DNA]
               (one phrase)
          [Brand Personality]
         (tone, character traits)
       [Brand Benefits — Emotional]
      (how using the brand makes you feel)
    [Brand Benefits — Functional]
   (what the brand practically does for you)
  [Brand Attributes / Features]
 (specific product/service characteristics)
```

**How it works**: Start at the base (what the product does) and build upward toward the emotional and symbolic reasons customers choose it. The brand essence at the top should be distilled from all layers below.

**Example — Nike**:
- Attributes: High-performance sports gear, innovative technology
- Functional benefits: Helps you run faster, train harder, recover better
- Emotional benefits: Feels empowering, builds confidence
- Personality: Inspiring, bold, athlete-minded
- Brand Essence: "Just Do It" — empowering human potential

**Workshop application**: Students build a brand pyramid for a digital brand, then evaluate whether the brand's digital marketing (ads, content, social) consistently expresses the top of the pyramid.

### 5.6 How Brand Strategy Drives Digital Marketing Decisions

1. **Content Strategy** — Brand personality and purpose determine content pillars and tone of voice
2. **Channel Selection** — Brand positioning determines which platforms fit (luxury brands avoid aggressive discounting platforms; youth brands prioritize TikTok)
3. **Targeting & Audience** — Brand positioning guides audience segmentation and persona development
4. **Visual Language** — Brand identity system ensures consistency across all digital assets
5. **Partnerships & Influencers** — Brand associations determine which influencers align with brand values
6. **Customer Experience** — Brand promise sets the standard for every digital touchpoint (website UX, chatbot tone, email design)
7. **Crisis Response** — Brand values guide how the brand communicates during controversies on social media

## 6. Key Relationships Between Frameworks

### Framework Hierarchy for Teaching

```
BRAND STRATEGY
(upstream, foundational — who are we?)
        |
        v
DIGITAL STRATEGY
(org-wide — how do digital technologies transform our business?)
        |
        v
DIGITAL MARKETING STRATEGY
(marketing-level — how do we use digital to attract, convert, retain?)
        |
        v
PLANNING FRAMEWORK: SOSTAC
(how do we create our digital marketing plan?)
    S → O → S → T → A → C
                    |
                    v
        EXECUTION FRAMEWORK: RACE
        (what activities do we execute across the customer lifecycle?)
            Reach → Act → Convert → Engage
```

### Integrated Workshop Flow

For a postgraduate Digital Marketing Strategy course, the frameworks can be taught in this sequence and integrated in a single semester project:

1. **Week 1**: Digital Strategy vs. Digital Marketing Strategy (definitions, distinctions)
2. **Week 2**: Brand Strategy — brand pyramid, Aaker equity model, CVP as brand expression
3. **Week 3**: Customer Value Proposition — Value Proposition Canvas workshop
4. **Week 4**: SOSTAC — situation analysis workshop (PESTEL + digital audit)
5. **Week 5**: SOSTAC — objectives, strategy, tactics (using RACE as tactics framework)
6. **Week 6**: RACE — deep dive into each stage, KPIs, channel planning
7. **Week 7**: Full integrated plan development (SOSTAC + RACE + CVP + Brand Strategy)
8. **Week 8**: Presentations — student teams present full digital marketing strategies
