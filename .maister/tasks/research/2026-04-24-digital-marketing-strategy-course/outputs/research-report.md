## Executive Summary

This report provides a complete, actionable teaching guide for an 8-hour postgraduate workshop day on Digital Marketing Strategy. The course covers five interconnected frameworks: Digital Strategy, Brand Strategy, Customer Value Proposition (CVP), SOSTAC, and RACE. It is designed for delivery in Polish to postgraduate e-marketing students with mixed levels of professional experience.

The report integrates findings from five research streams: framework theory (PR Smith, Dave Chaffey, Osterwalder), global and Polish market statistics (DataReportal 2025, Gemius/PBI/IAB Polska), five international case studies (Nike, Spotify, IKEA, Duolingo, Zalando), five Polish case studies (Allegro, Żabka, InPost, CD Projekt Red, mBank), and evidence-based course design principles (Knowles' andragogy, 70:20:10 model, Canva Whiteboard facilitation).

Key conclusions: (1) the five frameworks form a deliberate hierarchy — not alternatives but sequential layers — and this hierarchy should be the organizing principle of the entire day; (2) Polish case studies are analytically equivalent to international ones and more contextually relevant for this audience; (3) running all four Canva Whiteboard exercises on a single brand per group produces a near-complete digital marketing plan as the day's artifact; (4) approximately 2–2.5 hours of direct instruction and 4–5 hours of workshop activity is the evidence-based target ratio.

## SECTION 1: Recommended 8-Hour Course Agenda

### Design Principles Applied

The agenda follows three evidence-based principles:
- **Cognitive load**: Complex analytical content in the morning (high alertness); creative/collaborative exercises in the afternoon (natural re-energization post-lunch)
- **Hierarchy-first pedagogy**: Teaching the most concrete tools (SOSTAC) before the most abstract concepts (Brand Strategy/CVP), then revealing how the abstract concepts are the foundation — this is counterintuitive but works better for adult learners than pure top-down conceptual ordering
- **70:20:10 ratio**: Approximately 70% active learning (exercises, discussion), 20% peer learning (group work, gallery walks), 10% formal instruction (lecture)

### Full Day Schedule

```
09:00 – 09:15   Welcome and framing (15 min)
09:15 – 09:45   Icebreaker: "Brand You Trust / Brand You Don't" on Canva Whiteboard (30 min)
09:45 – 10:30   LECTURE BLOCK 1: Framework hierarchy + SOSTAC deep dive (45 min)
10:30 – 11:00   WORKSHOP 1: SOSTAC Situation Audit — Canva Whiteboard (30 min)
11:00 – 11:15   BREAK (15 min)
11:15 – 11:50   LECTURE BLOCK 2: RACE Framework + customer journey KPIs (35 min)
11:50 – 12:30   WORKSHOP 2: RACE Customer Journey Mapping — Canva Whiteboard (40 min)
12:30 – 13:30   LUNCH (60 min)
13:30 – 13:50   Re-energizer: "Good or Bad Campaign?" quickfire (20 min)
13:50 – 14:25   LECTURE BLOCK 3: Brand Strategy + CVP (35 min)
14:25 – 15:15   WORKSHOP 3: Value Proposition Canvas — Canva Whiteboard (50 min)
15:15 – 15:30   BREAK (15 min)
15:30 – 15:55   LECTURE BLOCK 4: Digital channel mix + Polish market context (25 min)
15:55 – 16:35   WORKSHOP 4: Budget Challenge / Channel Matrix — Canva Whiteboard (40 min)
16:35 – 17:00   Synthesis, reflections, Q&A, next steps (25 min)
```

**Total**: 8 hours exactly
- Direct instruction: ~2 hours 20 minutes
- Workshop / active exercises: ~2 hours 40 minutes
- Breaks + lunch: ~1 hour 30 minutes
- Transitions, facilitation, debrief: ~30 minutes

### Rationale for Module Sequencing

**SOSTAC before Brand Strategy/CVP**: SOSTAC is the most tangible and immediately applicable framework. Starting with it gives students a concrete planning scaffold. Brand Strategy and CVP are introduced in the afternoon as the strategic "why" behind SOSTAC — students then understand them in context.

**RACE immediately after SOSTAC**: RACE is SOSTAC's execution layer. Teaching them in sequence (morning) allows students to see the integration explicitly. Their afternoon exercises can then apply both simultaneously.

**Brand Strategy + CVP in the afternoon**: These are the most conceptually rich topics. The afternoon timing uses the natural post-lunch energy re-build (post re-energizer). The Value Proposition Canvas exercise is the most intensive workshop of the day — it needs the full 50 minutes the afternoon slot provides.

**Channel mix last**: Channel planning is the most concrete and decision-oriented topic. It works well at end-of-day when students want practical, actionable outputs they can take away.

### Polish-Specific Timing Note

Polish postgraduate sessions often run on a slightly loose 9:00 start (participants settling in from commute). Build a soft 5-minute buffer into the 09:00 welcome. If the cohort is meeting for the first time, extend the icebreaker to 45 minutes — relationship building is crucial for afternoon group work quality.

## SECTION 2: Introduction Module — Digital Strategy, Brand Strategy, CVP

### What to Cover

This module sets the conceptual stage for the entire day. Students must leave it understanding three things:
1. The three concepts are not synonyms — they operate at different levels and answer different questions
2. They form a hierarchy: Brand Strategy is most upstream; Digital Marketing Strategy is most operational
3. Everything they learn today (SOSTAC, RACE, channel planning) fits somewhere in this hierarchy

### Key Definitions and Distinctions

**Digital Strategy** (organizational level):
"An organization's priority initiatives for future investment in digital technology to make a business more competitive by digitalization." (Dave Chaffey)
- Owner: C-suite (CTO, CEO, CDO)
- Scope: Entire organization — IT, products, operations, HR, marketing
- Timeframe: Multi-year transformation
- Polish example: mBank's decision to operate without physical branches from 2000 was a digital strategy decision, not a marketing decision

**Digital Marketing Strategy** (marketing level):
"Defines the focus of digital marketing efforts to improve the contribution digital makes to an organization." (Dave Chaffey)
- Owner: CMO, Head of Digital Marketing
- Scope: Marketing function — attracting, converting, retaining customers digitally
- Timeframe: Annual planning cycle
- Polish example: Allegro's decision to invest in emotional YouTube brand films rather than price-led advertising

**Brand Strategy** (identity level):
"A tool that helps organizations better understand who they are and create a plan to communicate this to others." (Indeed.com)
- Owner: Brand/Creative Director, CEO
- Scope: Brand identity, values, purpose, positioning, personality
- Timeframe: Long-term (5–10+ years, relatively stable)
- Polish example: CD Projekt Red's "no DRM, free DLC, player-first" philosophy — a brand strategy decision that shaped all marketing

**Customer Value Proposition (CVP)**:
"A concise statement that communicates the unique benefits a product/service delivers to a specific target customer, explaining why that customer should choose this offering over alternatives." (Shopify)
- CVP vs. USP: USP answers "What makes us different?" — CVP answers "Why should the customer care?"
- CVP is brand strategy translated into customer language
- Polish example: InPost's CVP — "Your package, on your terms, at any hour, 7 minutes from your home"

### The Hierarchy Summary (Recommend as Visual on Slide)

```
BRAND STRATEGY
"Who are we and what do we stand for?" (5–10+ years)
        |
        v
DIGITAL STRATEGY
"How do digital technologies transform our business?" (multi-year)
        |
        v
DIGITAL MARKETING STRATEGY
"How do we use digital to attract, convert, and retain customers?" (annual)
        |
        v
PLANNING FRAMEWORK: SOSTAC
(How do we build the plan?)
        |
        v
EXECUTION FRAMEWORK: RACE
(How do we execute it across the customer lifecycle?)
```

### Recommended Slide Structure (10–12 slides)

1. Title: "Zanim zaczniesz planować — czym jest strategia cyfrowa?" (Before you plan — what is digital strategy?)
2. The three-level hierarchy diagram (visual)
3. Digital Strategy: definition + mBank as example
4. Digital Marketing Strategy: definition + Allegro as example
5. Brand Strategy: definition + the "who are we?" question + CD Projekt Red as example
6. CVP: definition + CVP vs. USP table + InPost as example
7. The key insight: "All of today's frameworks are tools that serve this hierarchy"
8. [Optional] The Aaker Brand Equity Model — 5 dimensions with digital metrics
9. [Optional] The Brand Pyramid (attributes → essence) — Nike example
10. Transition slide to SOSTAC: "Now we have the hierarchy — let's learn the planning tool"

### Key Talking Points for Presenter

**On Digital vs. Digital Marketing Strategy**: "Wyobraźcie sobie, że jesteście CEO Żabki. Decyzja o budowie sieci automatycznych sklepów Nano to strategia cyfrowa — dotyczy całej organizacji, logistyki, HR, technologii. Decyzja o tym, żeby promować aplikację Żappka na TikToku — to już strategia marketingu cyfrowego." (Imagine you are the CEO of Żabka. The decision to build a network of autonomous Nano stores is a digital strategy — it affects the entire organization: logistics, HR, technology. The decision to promote the Żappka app on TikTok — that is a digital marketing strategy.)

**On Brand Strategy**: "Strategia marki odpowiada na pytanie: gdyby nasza firma zniknęła z rynku jutro, czy ktoś by jej naprawdę brakowało? Jeśli nie — nie mamy strategii marki, mamy tylko ofertę." (Brand strategy answers the question: if our company disappeared from the market tomorrow, would anyone genuinely miss it? If not — we don't have a brand strategy, we only have a price list.)

**On CVP**: "CVP to nie hasło reklamowe. To odpowiedź na jedno konkretne pytanie klienta: 'Dlaczego właśnie Wy, a nie ktoś inny?' Ta odpowiedź musi być konkretna, musi mówić o kliencie — nie o produkcie." (CVP is not an advertising slogan. It is the answer to one specific customer question: 'Why you and not someone else?' That answer must be specific, and it must talk about the customer — not the product.)

## SECTION 3: SOSTAC Module

### Full Teaching Guide

SOSTAC is a systematic planning methodology created by PR Smith. It is one of the most widely used marketing planning frameworks globally, with certified practitioners in dozens of countries. The name is an acronym: Situation, Objectives, Strategy, Tactics, Actions, Control.

**The core insight**: Most marketing plans jump from "where we are" (Situation) directly to "what we'll do" (Tactics). SOSTAC inserts three critical stages between diagnosis and execution: Objectives (where specifically do we want to be?), Strategy (the high-level direction for getting there), and then Actions and Control to ensure execution and learning.

### How to Explain Each of the 6 Components

**S — Situation Analysis** ("Gdzie jesteśmy?")
This is the diagnostic stage — the foundation. PR Smith estimates it should represent half of all planning effort. Four areas to analyze:
- Customer analysis: Who buys from us? Why? How? (build personas)
- Competitor analysis: Direct competitors + new digital entrants
- Company analysis: Internal capabilities, past performance, data assets
- External (PESTEL): Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental, Legal

Digital additions: Website analytics (traffic, bounce rate, conversion rate), social media audit, SEO performance, digital competitor benchmarking.

Teaching tip: Use InPost as the Situation example. Their situation in 2009: Polish e-commerce was growing but home delivery was frustrating (missed deliveries, time windows). Situation identified the customer pain. The entire Paczkomat strategy followed from that diagnosis.

**O — Objectives** ("Gdzie chcemy być?")
Objectives span from organizational vision to specific KPIs. All objectives must be SMART (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-bound). Common digital objectives: website traffic growth, leads generated, email subscribers, social media reach, e-commerce conversion rate, customer acquisition cost.

Teaching tip: Link to RACE stages. Best practice is to set at least one KPI per RACE stage — a Reach KPI, an Act KPI, a Convert KPI, and an Engage KPI. This prevents the common mistake of setting only sales objectives while ignoring awareness and loyalty metrics.

**S — Strategy** ("Jak tam dotrzemy — na poziomie ogólnym?")
The shortest section but the most critical. Strategy makes directional choices that filter all subsequent tactical decisions. PR Smith's TOPPP SEED framework covers: Target markets and positioning, Objectives and partnerships, Processes, Stages/Sequence, Engagement, Experience, and Data.

In practice, strategy answers: Which customer segments to prioritize? Which channels to lead with? How to differentiate from competitors? Which RACE stages need most investment?

Teaching tip: Nike's CDA strategy was: "Shift from wholesale to DTC digital, build membership as CVP, invest in first-party data." This one strategic statement eliminated 40+ wholesale partners and built four apps. That is strategy — not a channel list.

**T — Tactics** ("Jakich konkretnych narzędzi używamy?")
Tactics are the specific digital channels, content formats, and tools used to execute strategy. They map directly to RACE stages (this is where SOSTAC and RACE connect): Reach tactics (SEO, PPC, social media, influencer), Act tactics (content, email, landing pages), Convert tactics (CRO, retargeting, promotions), Engage tactics (email automation, loyalty programs, community).

Teaching tip: "Tactics without strategy is just noise. Strategy without tactics is just a wish." Show how the same tactic (e.g., TikTok content) serves different purposes depending on strategic context — Duolingo uses TikTok for Reach and brand building; a retailer might use TikTok Shop for Convert.

**A — Actions** ("Kto, co, kiedy, za ile?")
Actions ensure implementation through:
- Clear ownership (who is responsible for each tactic?)
- Budget allocation (how much goes where?)
- Timescales (what is the publishing calendar?)
- Skills and resources (what capabilities do we need?)

The 4Ms of Action: Men and women (talent), Money (budget), Minutes (time), Megadata (analytics and insights).

Teaching tip: "A plan without an owner is a wish. A strategy without a budget is a dream." Use the Budget Challenge exercise (Workshop 4) to make this tangible.

**C — Control** ("Jak mierzymy i dostosowujemy?")
Control closes the loop back to Situation, enabling agile planning. It establishes:
- What metrics to measure (KPIs per channel and RACE stage)
- Measurement frequency (weekly dashboard, monthly review, quarterly plan)
- Response protocols (what happens when a KPI falls below target?)
- Feedback loops (how do Control findings update the next Situation Analysis?)

The 90-day sprint model: SOSTAC works as 90-day mini-cycles — plan, execute, measure, adjust. This creates an agile marketing system rather than an annual static plan.

Teaching tip: "Control isn't the last step — it's the first step of the next plan." Return to this at the end of Workshop 1 when discussing what KPIs groups would track for their chosen brand.

### Workshop Exercise Design: SOSTAC Situation Audit

**Duration**: 30 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Tool**: Canva Whiteboard — SOSTAC grid (6 columns, one per stage)

Setup before session:
- Create 6 labeled zones on the Canva Whiteboard (Situation / Objectives / Strategy / Tactics / Actions / Control)
- Pre-assign sticky note colors per section (Situation = blue, Objectives = green, Strategy = yellow, Tactics = orange, Actions = pink, Control = purple)
- Pre-fill 1–2 example sticky notes per section using Allegro as the worked example

Facilitation steps:
1. (3 min) Show the Allegro worked example — "This is what a completed SOSTAC board looks like"
2. (3 min) Groups choose or are assigned a brand (options: InPost, Żabka, mBank, Zalando Poland, or a brand from their own industry)
3. (15 min) TOGETHER ALONE — each person works in parallel, adding sticky notes to all 6 sections for their assigned brand. Emphasis: add your own notes even if someone else has already added something similar.
4. (7 min) Group consolidation — remove duplicates, add missing insights, highlight one key finding per section with a star or dot vote
5. (2 min) One representative shares the single most interesting finding with the room

Facilitator watch-points:
- Groups frequently get stuck on "Control" — prompt: "What metrics would the CEO of this brand check every Monday morning?"
- Groups frequently have weak "Strategy" sections — prompt: "Strategy is not a list of channels. What is the one big bet this brand is making? What are they prioritizing over other things?"
- If a group finishes early: extension question — "What would change in this SOSTAC if the brand's primary goal shifted from customer acquisition to customer retention?"

### Suggested Brands for SOSTAC Discussion

**Primary recommendation for the exercise**: Żabka (well-known to Polish students, rich digital SOSTAC story with the Żappka app and Nano stores)

**Alternative brands by category**:
- E-commerce: Allegro (dominant Polish example), Zalando (European fashion platform)
- Tech/entertainment: Spotify (personalization + data strategy), CD Projekt Red (community-first)
- Financial: mBank (digital pioneer), Revolut (challenger bank)
- B2B: LinkedIn (platform strategy), HubSpot (inbound methodology)

## SECTION 4: RACE Module

### Full Teaching Guide

The RACE Planning Framework was developed by Dr. Dave Chaffey in 2010. It maps the customer lifecycle from initial awareness through to long-term loyalty and advocacy. RACE stands for Reach, Act, Convert, Engage. It is designed to work alongside SOSTAC: if SOSTAC answers "how do we plan?", RACE answers "what do we execute across the customer journey?"

**Why RACE is different from a traditional funnel**: A traditional funnel is linear and one-directional (awareness → purchase). RACE acknowledges that Engage (loyalty) creates new Reach (advocacy) — it is a cycle, not a funnel. Engaged customers become brand advocates who expand the Reach stage organically.

### How to Explain Each of the 4 Stages

**R — Reach** ("Jak budujemy widoczność i świadomość marki?")
Building brand awareness and driving traffic across owned, earned, and paid media.

Channels: SEO, PPC/Google Ads, social media (organic + paid), content marketing, display advertising, influencer marketing, PR, YouTube, podcasts.

KPIs:
- Unique website visitors / sessions
- Impressions and organic reach
- Share of voice
- Follower growth (social media)
- Keyword rankings (SEO positions)
- Click-through rate (CTR) on ads
- Cost per click (CPC)

Polish market statistics for Reach:
- YouTube reaches 24.4M Poles monthly (Gemius/PBI) — the widest digital Reach channel in Poland
- TikTok: 13.8M monthly users; 53 minutes average daily session (highest of any platform)
- Organic search drives 53% of all trackable website traffic globally

Teaching example: Nike's CDA and the Reach trade-off. Nike dropped 40+ wholesale partners to focus on DTC. Their Convert and Engage improved dramatically. But Reach suffered — products became harder to discover in physical stores. Revenue fell in FY2025. "Reach is not just an awareness nice-to-have — it is the top of a revenue system. Shrink Reach and everything downstream shrinks too."

**A — Act (Interact)** ("Jak skłaniamy odwiedzających do interakcji?")
Encouraging meaningful interactions before purchase. The Act stage is about moving from "passive browser" to "engaged prospect."

Examples of Act-stage goals: reading a blog post, viewing a product, signing up for a newsletter, downloading a lead magnet, requesting a demo, adding a product to a basket.

KPIs:
- Pages per session / time on site
- Bounce rate (lower is better engagement)
- Email newsletter sign-ups
- Lead form completions
- Product page views
- "Added to basket" events
- Content downloads

Teaching example: IKEA's Act problem. With 4.6 billion website visits per year, IKEA had enormous Reach — but converting those visits to purchases was difficult because customers couldn't "experience" furniture online. IKEA Place (AR app) and IKEA Kreativ were Act-stage investments: tools to increase the depth and quality of interaction before purchase, reducing purchase hesitation.

**C — Convert** ("Jak zamieniamy zainteresowanie w transakcję?")
Completing the sale through online or offline channels. Importantly, Chaffey notes that conversion to sale "either in online and/or offline channels" is the objective — many journeys start digitally but convert offline.

KPIs:
- Conversion rate (% of visitors who purchase)
- Revenue / Average Order Value (AOV)
- Cost per acquisition (CPA)
- Online vs. offline conversion attribution
- Abandoned cart recovery rate
- Lead-to-customer conversion rate (B2B)

Polish market statistics for Convert:
- Mobile: 77% of e-commerce website visits via mobile devices (Shopify/Statista)
- BLIK: 68% (+5pp YoY) of Polish e-commerce payments — the fastest-growing payment method
- Parcel lockers: 81% of Polish online shoppers prefer parcel lockers for delivery — a Convert-stage friction reducer
- 84% of Poles say convenient parcel lockers make them more likely to shop online

Teaching example: Zalando's free returns policy as a Convert-stage investment. Free returns eliminate the primary risk of online fashion purchase (uncertainty about fit). Zalando modeled high return rates (30–50% in European fashion) not as a cost but as customer acquisition spend that improved conversion rates.

**E — Engage** ("Jak budujemy lojalność i przewagę życiową?")
Building long-term customer loyalty through continued communication and repeat interactions. The most underinvested RACE stage in most organizations.

KPIs:
- Customer Lifetime Value (CLV/LTV)
- Net Promoter Score (NPS)
- Repeat purchase rate
- Email open rates / click rates (retention campaigns)
- Social media shares and mentions (advocacy)
- Churn rate (inverse — lower is better)
- Customer satisfaction score (CSAT)

Teaching example: Spotify Wrapped as Engage-stage perfection. Wrapped turns a year of passive listening into an active identity-expression event. Users share their Wrapped publicly — converting the Engage stage into new Reach (advocacy). This is the RACE cycle at its best: Engage → Reach → new Act → new Convert.

### Integration with SOSTAC

This is the critical integration slide: show the RACE stages mapped onto SOSTAC:

| SOSTAC Component | RACE Integration |
|---|---|
| Situation | Audit current performance across all four RACE stages |
| Objectives | Set specific KPI targets for each RACE stage |
| Strategy | Decide which RACE stages to prioritize and how to differentiate |
| Tactics | List specific channels and content for each RACE stage |
| Actions | Assign ownership and budgets for each RACE-stage tactic |
| Control | Build a dashboard tracking KPIs for each RACE stage |

"SOSTAC is the planning framework. RACE is the execution map within that plan. You cannot do SOSTAC properly without RACE — and RACE without SOSTAC is just a list of activities without direction."

### Workshop Exercise Design: RACE Customer Journey Mapping

**Duration**: 40 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Tool**: Canva Whiteboard — horizontal swim lanes (one per RACE stage)

Setup:
- Four horizontal swim lanes labeled Reach / Act / Convert / Engage
- Each lane has space for: channels used, customer emotions, friction points, improvement opportunities
- Different sticky note color per stage

Facilitation steps:
1. (5 min) Show a worked example — use Żabka's customer journey mapped across RACE
2. (5 min) Groups agree on their brand and define a specific persona (age, goal, starting point)
3. (20 min) Map the journey:
   - Reach: How does the customer first discover this brand? (search, social, word-of-mouth, store visibility?)
   - Act: What actions do they take before deciding? (what content do they consume, what questions do they have?)
   - Convert: What converts them? What friction exists at the moment of purchase?
   - Engage: What brings them back? What builds loyalty beyond the first transaction?
4. (5 min) Groups add annotations in a contrasting color: "what's missing" or "what could be improved" at each stage
5. (5 min) Gallery walk — groups briefly explain their most important insight to one other group

KPIs per stage: After the mapping, ask groups to add one primary KPI to each swim lane (what would you measure to know if this stage is working?). This connects the exercise to Control in SOSTAC.

Extension question for advanced groups: "Which RACE stage is your chosen brand most under-investing in? What would you recommend they do differently?"

## SECTION 5: Case Studies Guide

### When and How to Use Case Studies

Case studies should be used in two ways throughout the day:

1. **As lecture examples** (2–3 minutes each): Brief, illustrative reference during a framework explanation. Does not require class discussion. Used to make the framework concrete.

2. **As discussion anchors** (10–15 minutes): A case is presented with 1–2 discussion questions, then groups discuss briefly before the facilitator harvests insights. Best used at the end of a lecture block before transitioning to a workshop.

**Recommended pacing**: 2–3 cases as lecture examples, 2–3 cases as discussion anchors. Do not try to cover all 10 in depth — select based on which frameworks need illustration most.

### International Case Studies

#### Nike — Consumer Direct Acceleration (CDA)

**Summary for presentation**: Nike had 65%+ of revenue through wholesale partners in 2019. CEO John Donahoe launched the "Consumer Direct Acceleration" strategy: pivot to DTC digital, build a membership ecosystem (NikePlus), and cut 40+ wholesale accounts. By FY2023, NIKE Direct revenue reached $21.3B (~44–49% of total revenue) and NikePlus had 160M+ members. However, in FY2024–25 total revenue fell to $46.3B (-9.84%) as the company reversed course and re-engaged wholesale partners.

**Link to SOSTAC/RACE/CVP**:
- SOSTAC: Full plan — Situation (wholesale dependency), Objectives (DTC >50%), Strategy (membership ecosystem), Tactics (SNKRS app, NTC, NRC), Actions (40+ accounts dropped), Control (NIKE Direct % of revenue as primary KPI)
- RACE: Excellent illustration of Reach trade-off — sacrificing Reach for Convert and Engage
- CVP: NikePlus membership CVP — personalized access, exclusive content, community belonging

**Best use in day**: During SOSTAC lecture as full worked example; during RACE lecture for the Reach stage discussion question ("was it worth sacrificing Reach for Engage?")

**Discussion questions**:
1. Nike dropped over 40 wholesale partners to pursue DTC purity. Using the RACE framework, analyze what Nike gained in the Act and Engage phases — and what it lost in Reach. Was the trade-off worth it?
2. In 2024–2025, Nike's revenue declined and it re-engaged wholesale partners. Does this represent a strategic failure, a correction, or a maturation of the strategy?

### Exercise 1: SOSTAC Situation Audit

**Teaching goal**: Understand the Situation component of SOSTAC; practice applying PESTEL + competitive digital audit; identify gaps in own knowledge about a brand.

**Duration**: 30 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Position in day**: 10:30–11:00 (immediately after SOSTAC lecture)
**Tool**: Canva Whiteboard — SOSTAC 6-column grid

**Facilitator Setup**:
- Create a grid with 6 columns: S (Situation) / O (Objectives) / S (Strategy) / T (Tactics) / A (Actions) / C (Control)
- Alternatively: for a 30-minute exercise, focus only on S (Situation) in depth, with other columns labeled but only lightly filled
- Pre-fill the Situation column with a Żabka example: 2–3 sticky notes showing Customer insight, Competitor note, Company note, PESTEL note

**Step-by-Step Facilitation**:

Step 1 — Orient (3 minutes):
Show the pre-filled Żabka example on the Canva Whiteboard. Say: "This is what a completed Situation column looks like. Your task is to build a similar analysis for your chosen brand. The notes should be short — 1–2 sentences each. We're going for breadth, not depth, in 15 minutes."

Step 2 — Solo work (15 minutes):
Each group member works in parallel on their own sticky notes. Timer visible on screen. Assign each person a different area: Person A = Customer analysis; Person B = Competitor analysis; Person C = Company analysis; Person D = PESTEL. Person E (if 5 in group) = SOSTAC sections O through C (light touch on the other 5 columns).

Step 3 — Group consolidation (7 minutes):
- Remove obvious duplicates (drag and overlap)
- Identify the 1–2 most interesting or surprising insights per section
- Mark these with a dot sticker (Canva voting sticker)

Step 4 — Share-out (5 minutes):
One representative per group shares their single most interesting finding with the room. Facilitator captures emerging cross-group themes in the Facilitation Zone.

**Debrief Questions**:
- "What was hardest to analyze — Customer, Competitor, Company, or PESTEL? Why?"
- "What information would you need to make the Situation analysis more reliable? Where would you find it?"
- "If the Situation is the diagnostic — what diagnosis does this brand's situation suggest for their marketing strategy?"

**Common Problems and Fixes**:
- Groups stuck on Control: Prompt — "What metrics would the marketing director of this brand check on Monday morning?"
- Groups treating Strategy as a tactics list: Prompt — "Strategy answers 'what big bets are we making?' not 'what channels do we use?'"
- Groups too slow: Remind them the goal is breadth — 3–5 sticky notes per section is enough, not exhaustive analysis

### Exercise 2: RACE Customer Journey Mapping

**Teaching goal**: Apply the four RACE stages to a real brand; identify friction points and improvement opportunities at each stage; connect stages to KPIs.

**Duration**: 40 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Position in day**: 11:50–12:30 (after RACE lecture)
**Tool**: Canva Whiteboard — horizontal swim lanes

**Facilitator Setup**:
- Four horizontal swim lanes: Reach / Act / Convert / Engage
- Within each lane: three zones — Channels Used, Customer Emotions, Friction Points
- Color coding: Reach = blue stickies; Act = green; Convert = orange; Engage = red
- Pre-fill with Żappka (Żabka app) example across all four stages

**Step-by-Step Facilitation**:

Step 1 — Orient and persona (5 minutes):
Introduce the worked Żappka example. Then ask each group to define their customer persona in two sentences: "We are mapping the journey of [Anna, 32, Warsaw, works in marketing, shops online 2x/week, has used parcel lockers]." Write the persona name at the top of their group zone.

Step 2 — Mapping (20 minutes):
Groups work through each RACE stage, adding sticky notes for:
- Reach: How does this persona first discover the brand? (SEO, social, word-of-mouth, offline?)
- Act: What do they do next? What content do they consume? What questions do they have?
- Convert: What converts them from browsing to buying? What might stop them (friction)?
- Engage: What happens after purchase? What brings them back?
Encourage groups to think about the journey as a story: "Tell me what happens on a Tuesday when Anna is thinking about buying a jacket..."

Step 3 — Improvement annotations (5 minutes):
Using a contrasting color (yellow), groups add "What's missing?" sticky notes at each stage. "If you were CMO of this brand, what one thing would you change at each RACE stage?"

Step 4 — KPI layer (5 minutes):
Ask groups to add one primary KPI sticky note at each stage — what single metric would tell you if this stage is working?

Step 5 — Gallery walk (5 minutes):
Groups rotate (or present) to share their single most interesting "What's missing?" observation with one other group.

**Debrief Questions**:
- "Which RACE stage is your brand weakest at right now? What evidence do you have for that?"
- "Where in the journey does the customer experience the most friction? Is it a digital or physical friction point?"
- "If you could only invest in improving one RACE stage, which would give the highest ROI? Why?"

**Extension for advanced groups**: "For B2B brands, the RACE journey is typically longer and involves multiple stakeholders. How would your journey map change if the customer was a procurement team rather than an individual?"

### Exercise 3: Value Proposition Canvas

**Teaching goal**: Apply the Osterwalder Value Proposition Canvas to articulate a brand's CVP; practice customer-first thinking (Profile before Map); identify gaps between what customers need and what the brand currently offers.

**Duration**: 50 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Position in day**: 14:25–15:15 (after Brand Strategy + CVP lecture)
**Tool**: Canva Whiteboard — Value Proposition Canvas template (circle + square)

**Facilitator Setup**:
- Use the standard VPC layout: Customer Profile circle on the right (Jobs / Pains / Gains); Value Map square on the left (Products & Services / Pain Relievers / Gain Creators)
- Color coding: Customer Profile — Jobs = yellow; Pains = red; Gains = green. Value Map — Products = grey; Pain Relievers = orange; Gain Creators = blue
- Pre-fill with an InPost example: a few sticky notes showing how InPost's Customer Profile looks for a persona of "online shopper in Warsaw who misses home deliveries"
- **Critical setup rule**: Cover the Value Map side of the canvas with a "DO NOT START HERE" label. Students must complete the Customer Profile BEFORE touching the Value Map.

**Step-by-Step Facilitation**:

Step 1 — Explain and model (5 minutes):
Show the InPost worked example. Emphasize: "We always start with the customer — not with what we sell. You cannot build a good CVP if you start by listing product features. The features should only appear after you deeply understand what the customer is trying to accomplish."

Step 2 — Choose segment (2 minutes):
Each group defines one specific customer segment for their chosen brand. Be specific: not "online shoppers" but "Polish women aged 25–35 who shop for fashion online, are concerned about sustainability, and return products frequently."

Step 3 — Customer Profile — TOGETHER ALONE (10 minutes):
Solo work. Each person adds sticky notes to:
- Customer Jobs: What is this customer trying to accomplish? (functional, social, emotional jobs — "I want to look professional in a client meeting," "I want to feel sustainable about my purchases")
- Customer Pains: What frustrations, risks, and obstacles do they face? ("I can't tell if the size fits," "I hate waiting for delivery windows")
- Customer Gains: What outcomes or benefits do they want? ("I want to look great without spending hours researching," "I want next-day delivery that doesn't require me to be home")

Step 4 — Rank and prioritize (5 minutes):
Group votes on the top 3 pains and top 3 gains. Use Canva dot voting or ask each person to put a star on their top choice. The ranked items will anchor the Value Map.

Step 5 — Value Map — TOGETHER ALONE (10 minutes):
Solo work. Now each person adds sticky notes to:
- Products & Services: What does the brand actually offer?
- Pain Relievers: How does the brand address the specific pains ranked in Step 4?
- Gain Creators: How does the brand create the specific gains ranked in Step 4?

Step 6 — Gap analysis (10 minutes):
Groups compare: "Where are there pains on the Customer Profile that have NO matching Pain Reliever on the Value Map? Where are there gains the customer wants that the brand is not creating? These gaps are strategic opportunities."

Step 7 — Share (8 minutes):
Each group shares one gap or opportunity with the room. Facilitator notes patterns across groups.

**Debrief Questions**:
- "Did you start filling in the Value Map before finishing the Customer Profile? What did you notice about the quality of your CVP when you did that?"
- "Which of the gaps you identified is most actionable? What would the brand need to do to close it?"
- "How would you translate the insights from this canvas into a single CVP statement — one headline sentence that this customer segment would immediately understand?"

**Polish terminology to use during exercise**:
- Kanwa Propozycji Wartości (Value Proposition Canvas)
- Profil Klienta (Customer Profile) — Zadania (Jobs), Bóle (Pains), Korzyści (Gains)
- Mapa Wartości (Value Map) — Produkty i Usługi (Products & Services), Uśmierzacze Bólu (Pain Relievers), Kreatory Korzyści (Gain Creators)

### Exercise 4: Budget Challenge / Digital Channel Matrix

**Teaching goal**: Practice allocating a marketing budget across channels; map channels to RACE stages; justify strategic trade-offs explicitly.

**Duration**: 40 minutes
**Group size**: 3–5 per group
**Position in day**: 15:55–16:35 (after digital channel mix lecture)
**Tool**: Canva Whiteboard — table/matrix

**Facilitator Setup**:
- Matrix with rows = channels (SEO/content, Paid Search/Google Ads, Paid Social/Meta, TikTok, Email Marketing, Influencer Marketing, Display/Programmatic, Website CRO, Other)
- Columns: RACE Stage Served (Reach/Act/Convert/Engage), Budget Allocation (PLN), Priority (High/Med/Low), Primary KPI
- Each group starts with the same hypothetical budget: 200,000 PLN for a 6-month digital campaign
- Pre-fill with one example row (e.g., Email Marketing: Engage stage, 20,000 PLN, High priority, Repeat purchase rate)

**Step-by-Step Facilitation**:

Step 1 — Brief (5 minutes):
"You are the digital marketing team for [your chosen brand]. Your budget for the next 6 months is 200,000 PLN. Allocate it. For each channel you choose, state which RACE stage it primarily serves and what KPI you will use to measure success."

Step 2 — Group work (20 minutes):
Groups fill in the matrix, debating priorities. Facilitator circulates and challenges with questions: "Why SEO over paid search? What does your brand's current organic ranking look like? For TikTok — what content will you create and who will create it?"

Step 3 — Present and justify (7 minutes):
Each group presents their top 3 allocation decisions and the reasoning. 90 seconds per group.

Step 4 — Facilitator synthesis (5 minutes):
Highlight interesting divergence across groups. Ask: "Three groups allocated differently — are any of you wrong, or are you just serving different strategic priorities? What does this teach us about channel planning?"

Step 5 — Reflection (3 minutes):
Each person writes one thing on a sticky note: "One channel I was surprised about learning is worth investing in, because..."

**Debrief Questions**:
- "Which RACE stage is consistently under-budgeted across groups? What does that tell us about typical marketing budget biases?"
- "Which channels are you most confident in? Which do you feel least equipped to execute well?"
- "If your budget was cut to 50,000 PLN, what would you keep? What does that tell you about your actual strategic priorities?"

### Presenter Notes Format and Style Guidance

Use this four-line format for every concept slide:

```
[SAY]: The spoken script — write out the first 2–3 sentences in full. These are the exact words
to open the slide. After that, bullet points of key talking points are fine.

[DO]: What to click, animate, or physically do on this slide. Include timing cues for animations.

[WATCH]: What to observe in the room. Signs of confusion, engagement, or expertise that
should change your pace or direction.

[TIME]: Target clock time to be on this slide. Format: "Target: 10:25am"
```

For workshop instruction slides, add:

```
[SETUP]: Any last-minute checks before opening the exercise (e.g., "confirm Canva link is on screen")
[DEBRIEF]: 2–3 specific questions to use during the share-out after the exercise
[EXTENSION]: One question for groups that finish early
```

### Lecture-to-Workshop Transitions in Keynote

Every transition from lecture to workshop follows a 3-slide sequence:
1. **Instruction slide** — dark background, high contrast, TASK/TIME/OUTPUT/TOOLS format
2. **Example slide** — shows a partially completed output (what "done" looks like)
3. **Active/timer slide** — shows the Canva QR code, timer (if using screen timer), and a one-line reminder of the task

Every transition from workshop back to lecture follows:
1. **"Welcome back" slide** — distinct visual style; opens the debrief
2. **Debrief synthesis slide** — facilitator can live-type or annotate what they heard from groups
3. **Bridge slide** — explicitly connects what was just done to the next framework ("You just built a RACE map. Now let's talk about what drives the strategy behind it — Brand Strategy and CVP.")

## SECTION 9: Key Gaps and Uncertainties

### What Could Not Be Verified

1. **IAB Polska AdEx 2024 — channel-level breakdown**: The Polish digital advertising spend total (9.2B PLN) is available, but the breakdown by channel (search/social/video/display) was not accessible from the primary IAB Polska source during research. Before the course, the facilitator should download the full Raport Strategiczny IAB Polska 2024 from iabpolska.pl and verify current channel split data.

2. **Polish influencer marketing spend in PLN**: Only global figures are available ($24B globally). For Polish-specific data, consult the IAB Polska report or Influencer Marketing Hub country-level data.

3. **AI adoption rates in Polish marketing**: The 89% generative AI adoption figure is from the Content Marketing Institute (primarily US/global). Polish-specific AI marketing adoption data was not found. Facilitate the AI slide with appropriate caveats: "Globally, 89% of marketers use AI tools — in Poland this figure is likely lower but growing rapidly."

4. **Current (2026) Polish campaigns**: All research was conducted in April 2026, but the most recent Polish campaigns referenced are from 2024–2025. The facilitator should add 1–2 examples of campaigns running in Q1-Q2 2026. Recommended sources: marketingprzykawie.pl, Marketer+ (marketerplus.pl), Social Press (socialpress.pl), Brief.pl.

5. **LinkedIn Poland precision**: Two different figures exist for LinkedIn Poland — 8.20M (DataReportal advertising reach) and approximately 5M (Gemius monthly users). Present both with source context rather than choosing one definitive number.

6. **Zalando ecommerce share within total revenue**: The "~7% of revenue" figure is an industry estimate, not official Zalando disclosure. Use with explicit caveat: "industry estimates suggest approximately..."

### Where the Facilitator Should Add Their Own Material

1. **Industry-specific examples**: If the student cohort is concentrated in a specific industry (FMCG, finance, B2B tech, retail), prepare one SOSTAC and one RACE example from that industry specifically. The research covers a broad mix, but having a domain-matched example significantly increases engagement.

2. **Student work experience**: In the icebreaker, ask participants to briefly share their industry. Adjust examples and discussion questions accordingly throughout the day.

3. **B2B RACE examples**: The research focuses primarily on B2C cases. If the cohort has significant B2B experience, add: LinkedIn as a B2B Reach channel (85% of B2B marketers cite it as highest-ROI), gated content as Act-stage (lead magnets, whitepapers), lead scoring as Convert-stage, account-based marketing for Engage. None of these are deeply covered in the current research.

4. **Budget exercise calibration**: The 200,000 PLN budget for Workshop 4 is a reasonable starting point. Adjust up or down based on what sizes of organization students work in (a solopreneur exercise might be 20,000 PLN; a corporate exercise might be 2,000,000 PLN).

5. **Pricing benchmarks**: Current CPC/CPM benchmarks for Google Ads and Meta Ads in Poland change quarterly. Before the course, check current benchmarks on WordStream or Semrush's advertising benchmarks reports.

### Methodological Limitations

- International case study financial data is from most recent reported periods (FY2023–FY2025 depending on the company's reporting calendar). Revenue trajectories are accurate as of early 2026 but will change.
- Social media platform user data changes monthly; figures cited are from January 2025 (DataReportal) and Q4 2024 (Gemius/PBI). For slides, include the data date.
- The 70:20:10 learning model is widely referenced in corporate L&D but has mixed empirical support in academic literature. Its utility here is as a practical design heuristic, not a rigorously validated learning science finding.
